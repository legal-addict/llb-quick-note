1.3 DEFINITION, REGISTRATION AND RECOGNITION

1. Definition of Trade Union (According to Section 2(h) of the Trade Unions Act, 1926)
 A trade union means any combination, whether temporary or permanent,

formed primarily for the purpose of regulating the relations between:
 - Workmen and employers
- Workmen and workmen
- Employers and employers
It also includes federations of trade unions.

Key Points:
 - It must be a combination of workers
- Main purpose is to protect or improve workers‚Äô interests
- Deals with wages, working hours, welfare, and employment conditions

2. Registration of Trade Unions
 - Registration is voluntary under the Trade Unions Act
- A registered union gets legal benefits and protection
- Unregistered unions do not get statutory protection

Procedure for Registration:
 - Application must be made to the Registrar of Trade Unions of the State
- Application must be signed by at least 7 members of the trade union

Documents Required:
 - Rules of the union (objectives, membership rules, office bearers)
- Names, addresses, and occupations of members

Certificate of Registration:
 - Issued by the Registrar
- Gives legal recognition to the trade union

Effects of Registration:
 - Registered union becomes a legal entity
- Can acquire property
- Can enter into contracts
- Can sue and be sued
- Protects members‚Äô interests more effectively

3. Recognition of Trade Unions
 Recognition is different from registration.

Meaning:
 - Recognition means the employer officially accepts a trade union as the representative of workers for collective bargaining

Key Points:
 - Usually granted to the union having majority membership
- Recognition allows negotiation on wages, hours, and working conditions

Types of Recognition:
 - Voluntary recognition by employer
- Statutory recognition under labour laws
(e.g., Industrial Disputes Act, 1947)

Effect of Recognition:
 - Recognized union gets exclusive rights for collective bargaining

1.4 IMMUNITIES IN TRADE DISPUTES: CRIMINAL AND CIVIL

1. Concept of Immunity in Trade Disputes
 - Immunity means protection from legal action
- Available to employees, trade unions, and workers
when they participate in trade disputes

Trade Dispute:
 - Dispute between employers and employees relating to:
wages, working conditions, employment terms, or rights

Immunity protects workers from:
 - Criminal liability (e.g., assault, trespass in certain cases)
- Civil liability (e.g., damages for loss caused to employer)

2. CRIMINAL IMMUNITY IN TRADE DISPUTES
 Workers and trade unions are generally immune from criminal liability
for acts done in contemplation or furtherance of a trade dispute,
under certain laws.

Key Points:
Protected acts may include:
- Strikes
- Picketing
- Refusal to work
- Protest against unfair labour practices

Legal Basis (India):
 Industrial Disputes Act, 1947
- Section 33: Workers are not criminally liable for acts committed in contemplation or furtherance of a trade dispute, provided the acts are done in good faith and are not excessive or violent.

Limits of Criminal Immunity:
- Acts of violence
- Damage to property
- Assault or intimidation

Such acts will attract criminal liability.
Immunity applies only when the act is directly related to the trade dispute.

3. CIVIL IMMUNITY IN TRADE DISPUTES
 Civil immunity protects workers and trade unions from civil suits,
such as claims for damages by employers.

Key Points:
 Protected acts include:
- Strikes
- Picketing
- Work stoppage
- Collective bargaining actions
Legal Provision: Under labour laws, no civil liability arises if the act is done in furtherance of a bona fide trade dispute.

Example:
 Employees going on a legal strike cannot be sued for loss of profits suffered by the employer.

Limits of Civil Immunity:
Civil liability may arise if:
 - The act is malicious or ultra vires (beyond legal limits)
- Damage caused is unrelated to the trade dispute

1.5 COLLECTIVE BARGAINING ‚Äì PURPOSE AND ITS TYPES

1. Definition of Collective Bargaining
 Collective bargaining is a process where employers and employees (usually represented by trade unions) negotiate terms and conditions of employment.
- It is a joint discussion to reach mutually acceptable agreements
- It helps maintain industrial harmony in the workplace

Key Features:
 - Between employer and employees (or trade union)
- Focuses on wages, working hours, benefits, and working conditions
- Negotiation results in a collective agreement

2. PURPOSE OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING

 The main objectives are:
 Improve Wages and Benefits
 - Ensure fair pay, bonuses, medical benefits, allowances

Protect Employee Rights
 - Job security
- Safety
- Grievance redressal

Maintain Industrial Harmony
 - Reduce strikes, lockouts, and industrial unrest

Mutual Consultation
 - Employers understand worker needs
- Workers understand company constraints

Increase Productivity
 - When workers are satisfied, efficiency improves

3. TYPES OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
 Collective bargaining can be classified based on scope, levels, or subject matter.

A. Based on Scope / Level

Industry-Level Bargaining (Macro-level)
 - Negotiation covers an entire industry
- Example: Wages of coal miners across the country

Enterprise-Level Bargaining (Micro-level)
 - Negotiation covers a single company or organization
- Example: Working hours or bonuses in a factory

B. Based on Nature of Negotiation

Conjunctive / Distributive Bargaining
Distributive bargaining is negotiation where parties divide existing resources, and gain of one is loss of the other.s
example: Company has limited money.
Workers ask for higher salary.
If salary increases ‚Üí company profit decreases.

Integrative / Interest-based Bargaining
 - Focuses on mutual gains and problem-solving
- Example: Introducing flexible working hours to benefit both workers and employer

C. Based on Subjects

 Wages / Salary Bargaining
 - Negotiating pay, allowances, overtime

Conditions of Work Bargaining
 - Safety, working hours, leave

Union Rights Bargaining
 - Recognition of union, grievance handling

4. BENEFITS OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
 - Reduces industrial disputes
- Promotes mutual trust between management and employees
- Ensures fair treatment of employees
- Improves motivation and productivity

1.6 COLLECTIVE BARGAINING PROCESS, ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES

1. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING ‚Äì DEFINITION

 Collective Bargaining is:
 ‚ÄúThe process of negotiation between employers and a group of employees (usually represented by a trade union) to determine working conditions, wages, benefits, and other terms of employment.‚Äù

Key Points:
 - Usually between employer and recognized trade union
- Aims to reach a mutually acceptable agreement
- Often results in a Collective Agreement (written contract)

2. PROCESS OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING

 The process generally involves five stages:
1. Preparation
 - Both employer and union assess demands, grievances, and goals
- Data collected on wages, working conditions, profits, etc.

2. Negotiation
 - Representatives of workers and employers meet and negotiate
- Demands and counter-demands are discussed

3. Proposals and Counter-Proposals
 - Each party makes offers and adjustments
- Emphasis on compromise and mutual understanding

4. Settlement / Agreement
 - Once consensus is reached, a formal Collective Agreement is drafted
- Agreement is signed by both parties
- Agreement is binding for the agreed period

5. Implementation and Review
 - Terms of the agreement are implemented at the workplace
- Periodic review for renewal or modification

3. ADVANTAGES OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
 - Promotes industrial peace and harmony
- Ensures fair wages and better working conditions
- Reduces industrial disputes and strikes
- Improves communication between workers and management
- Increases job satisfaction and productivity

4. DISADVANTAGES OF COLLECTIVE BARGAINING
 - Process can be time-consuming
- May lead to strikes if negotiations fail
- Sometimes dominated by strong unions
- May increase costs for employers
- Not effective where unions are weak

INDUSTRIAL DISPUTES ACT, 1947
1. INDUSTRY ‚Äì CONCEPTUAL ANALYSIS

Definition under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947:

 Section 2(j) defines ‚ÄúIndustry‚Äù as:
‚ÄúIndustry includes any systematic activity carried on by cooperation between employer and employee for the production, supply, or distribution of goods or services with a view to satisfy human wants or wishes (whether material or immaterial).‚Äù

KEY ELEMENTS OF THE DEFINITION

1. Systematic Activity
There must be an organized and continuous activity.
It should not be occasional or casual.
Example: A factory manufacturing textiles is a systematic activity.

2. Cooperation between Employer and Employee
Both employer and employees must participate.
Solo or self-employed activities do not constitute an industry.
Example: A shop employing workers qualifies, but a single person working alone does not.

3. Production, Supply, or Distribution
Includes goods and services.
Covers manufacturing and service sectors.
Examples: Banking, education, hospitals, IT services.

4. Satisfaction of Human Wants
The activity must aim to satisfy human needs.
Needs may be material or immaterial.
Example: Hospitals and hotels satisfy immaterial wants.

5. Employer‚ÄìEmployee Relationship
Essential requirement of an industry.
Without this relationship, an activity is not an industry.

JUDICIAL INTERPRETATION

Tata Iron & Steel Co. Ltd. v. Their Workmen (1963)
Court emphasized cooperation for productive activity as the key element of industry.

Bangalore Water Supply v. A. Rajappa (1978)
Industry was interpreted in a very broad sense.
Included public utility services like water supply.

Dabur India Ltd. Case
Service-oriented businesses also qualify as industry.
Employer‚Äìemployee cooperation is decisive.

2. CONCEPT ‚Äì INDUSTRIAL DISPUTE

Definition (Section 2(k) of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947):
‚ÄúIndustrial dispute means any dispute or difference between employers and employers, or between employers and workmen, or between workmen and workmen, which is connected with employment or non-employment, terms of employment, or conditions of labor of any person.‚Äù

KEY ELEMENTS OF INDUSTRIAL DISPUTE

1. Parties Involved
Employer vs Employer
Employer vs Workman
Workman vs Workman

2. Subject Matter
Employment or non-employment
Terms of employment (wages, hours, leave)
Conditions of labour (safety, discipline, working conditions)

Legal Consequence:
Industrial disputes may lead to conciliation, arbitration, or adjudication under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947.

Examples:
Workers demanding a wage hike.
Workers protesting unsafe working conditions.
Conflict between two groups of employees in a company.

2. Workman ‚Äì Concept

Definition (Section 2(s) of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947):
A workman means any person employed in an industry to do manual, skilled, technical, clerical, or operational work for hire or reward.

Key Points:

Inclusions:
Manual workers
Skilled workers
Technical employees
Clerical employees
Operational staff

Exclusions:
Managerial staff
Administrative staff
Persons employed in supervisory capacity having managerial powers (subject to court interpretation).

Examples:
Factory worker, clerk, technician, driver = Workman
Manager, director, CEO = Not a Workman

3. Employer ‚Äì Concept

Definition (Section 2(g) of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947):
‚ÄúEmployer includes the owner of the industry and any manager, agent, or person responsible for the management of the industry.‚Äù

Key Points:
Employer is responsible for employment and labour management.
Takes decisions related to wages, working conditions, and discipline.
May be an individual, company, or government body.

6. AWARD AND SETTLEMENT


AWARD
- Decision given by Labour Court, Industrial Tribunal, or Arbitrator is called award.
- Defined under Sec 2(b).
- Comes after judicial/quasi-judicial inquiry.
- Binding when published by the government under Sec 17.
- Applies to all parties involved in the dispute and sometimes to future workers (Sec 18(3)).
- Two types: Private Settlement (Sec 18(1)) ‚Äì binding only on signatories; Settlement in course of conciliation (Sec 18(3)) ‚Äì binding on all.
- Generally longer process (hearings, evidence, arguments).
- More formal and rigid.
- Enforceable like a court order.

SETTLEMENT
- Agreement reached between employer and workmen.
- Defined under Sec 2(p).
- Comes through negotiation & compromise.
- Binding when signed and sent to labour authorities.
- Faster and avoids litigation.
- More flexible and cooperative.
- Enforceable as a contractual agreement with legal backing.

MODULE 3: MRTU & PULP, 1971

1. Concept ‚Äì Industry, Labour Courts, Unfair Labour Practices etc.



1. Concept ‚Äì Industry
Meaning:
Any business, trade, manufacturing, service, or occupation where employers and employees work together.
Same understanding as ‚Äúindustry‚Äù under I.D. Act.
Includes public & private sector.
Excludes:
- Armed forces
- Police
- Domestic servants
- Charitable activities

2. Labour Courts (Under MRTU & PULP Act)
Purpose:
To decide cases related to Unfair Labour Practices (ULPs) of employers and trade unions.

Functions:
- Investigate complaints of ULPs.
- Grant relief (reinstatement, compensation, cease & desist orders).
- Enforce rights of workers under the Act.
- Power similar to a civil court.

Jurisdiction:
Matters listed under Item 1 of Schedule IV and other ULP complaints.
Unfair Labour Practices (ULP) Defined under Section 26.

A. ULP by Employers (Schedule II & Schedule IV)
Examples:
- Interfering in trade union activities.
- Victimization(cruel treatment like bullying) of employees.
- Wrongful dismissal or discharge.
- Not implementing settlement/award.
- Creating false contracts to avoid regular employment.
- Threatening employees.

B. ULP by Trade Unions of Workmen (Schedule III)
Examples:
- Intimidating (make afraid ) workers who do not join union.
- Coercive activities.
- Forcing employer to choose a particular union.
- Gheraos, violence, wrongful strikes.

C. ULP by Workmen
Examples:
- Go-slow.
- Unlawful strikes.
- Wilful damage to employer‚Äôs property.

4. Key Features of the MRTU & PULP Act, 1971
- Recognizes trade unions.
- Prevents unfair labour practices.
- Provides legal rights to workers.
- Ensures industrial peace.
- Empowers Labour & Industrial Courts.
- Makes ULPs punishable.
- Provides speedy remedy.

5. Penalties
- ULP is a criminal offence.
- Punishment: imprisonment, fine, or both (Section 30).
- Court can order reinstatement/compensation as relief.

2. Authorities within the Act

1. Registrar of Trade Unions
Role:
- Registers and recognizes trade unions.
- Cancels or renews registration.
- Ensures unions follow legal requirements.
- Decides disputes related to registration.

2. Investigating Officers
Role:
- Help Labour & Industrial Courts by collecting evidence.
- Inspect records, documents, workplaces.
- Submit reports on Unfair Labour Practices (ULPs).

3. Labour Court
Deals With:
- Unfair Labour Practices of employers (Schedule II & IV).
- Wrongful dismissals, victimization, illegal transfers.
- Non-implementation of settlements/awards.

Powers:
- Order reinstatement(giving someone back a position they have lost.), back wages, compensation.

4. Industrial Court
Deals With:
- Unfair Labour Practices of trade unions (Schedule III).
- Recognition of unions.
- Rival union disputes.
- Interpretation of settlements/awards.

Powers:
- Enforce recognition.
- Cancel or suspend union recognition.
- Prevent ULPs.

5. Recognized Unions
Role:
- Sole bargaining agent.
- Represent workers before courts & employers.
- Participate in negotiations, settlements, and agreements.

3. Recognition of Union and its rights with obligations



A. RECOGNITION OF UNION (Meaning + Process)
Meaning:
A union officially accepted by the employer and government as the sole bargaining agent for workers in an undertaking.

Criteria for Recognition (Industrial Court):
- Must have minimum 30% membership of total workers (continuous 6 months).
- Union should be democratic, independent, and functioning properly.
- No Unfair Labour Practices should be committed by the union.

B. RIGHTS OF A RECOGNIZED UNION
1. Sole Bargaining Agent
Only recognized union can negotiate settlements & agreements.

2. Represent Workers
- Act on behalf of workers in disputes.
- Represents workers before management, courts, and labour authorities.

3. Right to Collect Dues
- Subscription, union fees, welfare fund.
- these funds are vital for their representation functions

4. Right to Raise Industrial Disputes
- On behalf of all workers.

5. Right to Meet and Communicate
- Access to notice boards, meeting rooms, premises (with permission).

6. Protection from Discrimination
- Employer cannot victimise recognized union leaders.

C. OBLIGATIONS (Duties) OF RECOGNIZED UNION

1. No Unfair Labour Practices (ULP)
- Cannot coerce (pressure)workers.
- Cannot use violence.
- Cannot force membership.
- Cannot stop non-union workers from working.

2. Maintain Democratic Functioning
- Transparent elections.
- Proper accounts.
- Regular meetings.

3. Represent All Workers Fairly
- Union must protect all workers, not only members.

4. Promote Industrial Peace
- Cooperate with employer.
- Avoid unnecessary strikes.
- Solve disputes amicably.

5. Respect Law & Agreements
- Follow MRTU & PULP Act.
- Implement settlements/awards.
- Maintain discipline in workplace.

4. Illegal Strikes and Lockouts



Illegal Strike
- A strike started or continued in violation of the MRTU & PULP / Industrial Disputes Act.
- Workers stop work without following legal procedure.
- Strike without 14-day notice (in public utility services) is illegal.
- Strike during conciliation, arbitration, tribunal proceedings is illegal.
- Strike violating settlement/award is illegal.
- Workers may face wage deduction & disciplinary action.
- Covered under ULP Items of Schedule III & IV.

Illegal Lockout
- A lockout declared or continued in violation of the Act.
- Employer closes workplace without following legal procedure.
- Lockout without 14-day notice (public utility services) is illegal.
- Lockout during conciliation, arbitration, tribunal proceedings is illegal.
- Lockout violating settlement/award is illegal.
- Victimizing closure, coercive lockout ‚Üí considered unfair labour practice.
- Employer may face penalties, compensation orders.
- Covered under ULP Items of Schedule II & IV.

5. Unfair Labour Practices


Meaning:
- Any unfair action by employer, worker, or union.
- Listed in Schedules II, III, IV of MRTU & PULP Act, 1971.
- Violates rights of workers and affects industrial peace.

A. ULP by Employers (Schedule II & IV)
- Interfering with union activities.
- Threatening, coercing, or victimizing employees.
- Wrongful dismissal, discharge, or transfer.
- Refusal to bargain with recognized union.
- Creating bogus contracts to avoid permanent employment.
- Not implementing settlements/awards.
- Discriminating workers based on union membership.

B. ULP by Trade Unions of Workmen (Schedule III)
- Forcing workers to join union.
- Intimidating non-union workers.
- Coercive actions to force union recognition.
- Violent acts like gherao, assaults, threats.
- Preventing workers from entering workplace.

C. ULP by Workmen
- Go-slow (deliberately reducing output).
- Sit-down / stay-in strikes.
- Destruction of employer‚Äôs property.
- Illegal strikes.
- Wilful refusal to follow lawful orders.

6. Power of Courts and Penalties



A. Powers of Labour Court
- Inquire into Unfair Labour Practices (ULP) by employers & workmen.
- Order reinstatement of workers.
- Grant back wages, compensation, or other relief.
- Issue Cease-and-Desist Orders (stop the ULP immediately).
- Enforce settlements and awards.
- Summon witnesses, call documents, take evidence (civil court powers).
- Direct employer to withdraw wrongful actions (suspension, transfer, dismissal).
