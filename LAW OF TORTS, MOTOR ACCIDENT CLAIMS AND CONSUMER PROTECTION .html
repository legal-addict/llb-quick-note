1.1 Tort: Definition, Nature, Scope and object of Tort, Tort in India.


1. What is a Tort?
Tort means a civil wrong.

‚Ä¢ Someone has a duty to behave properly,
‚Ä¢ They break that duty,
‚Ä¢ Someone else suffers loss or injury,
‚Ä¢ And the victim can get compensation (money).

‚Ä¢ It is a civil wrong (person vs person).
‚Ä¢ The main result is compensation, not jail.
‚Ä¢ It is not written in one book (not a code).
‚Ä¢ It is made by judges through cases.
‚Ä¢ It protects private rights like body, property, reputation.

‚Ä¢ To give compensation to the victim.
‚Ä¢ To stop people from doing wrongful acts.
‚Ä¢ To protect rights of people (body, property, reputation).
‚Ä¢ To maintain peace by solving disputes peacefully.

1.3 Damnum Sine Injuria, Injuria Sine Damnum

1. Damnum Sine Injuria
Meaning:
Damage (loss) without legal injury.
You suffer loss, but no legal right is violated ‚Üí No tort, no compensation.

Example:
A opens a shop next to B.
B loses customers.
B suffers money loss, but A did nothing illegal.
‚Üí No compensation.

2. Injuria Sine Damnum
Meaning:
Legal injury without actual loss.
Your legal right is violated, even if you suffer no monetary loss.
‚Üí Tort exists, compensation possible.

Example:
A is stopped from entering a public park even though he has a right.
No money loss, but his legal right is violated.
‚Üí Tort.

1.4 Volenti non-fit injuria, Ubi jus ibi remedium

1. Volenti Non Fit Injuria
Meaning:
‚ÄúIf a person voluntarily agrees to a risk, they cannot complain later.‚Äù
Volenti = voluntarily
Non fit injuria = no injury (in law)

Simple idea:
If you knew the risk + accepted the risk, then no compensation.
Ex. Sports injuries
Players accept the risk of getting hurt.
‚Üí No tort if injury happens in normal play.

Important Conditions:
The person must have full knowledge of the risk.
The person must freely agree (no force).

2. Ubi Jus Ibi Remedium
Meaning:
‚ÄúWhere there is a right, there is a remedy.‚Äù
If your legal right is violated, the law gives you a solution/remedy.

Simple idea:
Every right has a protection.
If someone violates your rights, courts will help you.

Examples:
Right to property
Someone enters your land ‚Üí You can go to court.

Right to reputation
Someone spreads false rumours ‚Üí You can sue for defamation.

Right to move freely
Wrongly stopped by police ‚Üí You can claim compensation.

Right to vote
If someone illegally stops you ‚Üí You get a legal remedy.

When it does NOT apply:
This principle does not apply for:
Moral wrongs (hurting feelings without legal harm)
Political wrongs (no legal right violated)

1.5 Extinguishment of Liability in Tort

Meaning:
Extinguishment of liability means:
The tort liability ends ‚Üí the wrongdoer (defendant) no longer has to pay or face the claim.

In simple words:
When a tort case ends or the right to sue gets cancelled.
1. Death of Parties
If the wrongdoer or the injured person dies, in many tort cases the claim ends.

2. Waiver (Giving Up the Right)
The injured person chooses not to sue even when they can.
Example:
A was hurt by B‚Äôs negligence.
A says ‚ÄúI forgive you, I won‚Äôt take action.‚Äù
‚Üí A has waived the right ‚Üí liability ends.

3. Accord and Satisfaction (Settlement)
Both parties settle the matter outside court.
The injured person accepts money/compensation, and the issue is closed.

4. Limitation (Time Over)
Every tort has a time limit (normally 1‚Äì3 years).
If the injured person files the case after the time expires, liability ends.
Example:
A injured B on 1 Jan 2023.
Limitation = 1 year.
Case filed in Feb 2025 ‚Üí Too late ‚Üí No case.

5. Contributory Negligence
If the plaintiff is also responsible for the harm, compensation reduces or ends.
Example:
A and B both were careless while driving.
Accident happens.
Court says A was also 50% at fault ‚Üí B‚Äôs liability reduces.

6. Act of God (Natural Event)
If harm is caused by natural forces and no one is at fault ‚Üí No liability.
Example:
Tree falls on A‚Äôs car because of a sudden earthquake.
‚Üí Nobody is responsible ‚Üí No liability.

7. Statutory Authority
If the law allows someone to do an act, and harm occurs during that lawful act ‚Üí No liability.
Example:
Municipal corporation demolishes an illegal building under law.
Owner cannot sue for damage. ‚Üí Liability ends.

8. Plaintiff‚Äôs Default (Plaintiff‚Äôs own fault)
If injury happens because of the plaintiff‚Äôs own mistake, defendant not liable.
Example:
A ignores ‚ÄúHigh Voltage ‚Äì Danger‚Äù sign and touches the wire.
‚Üí A responsible for own injury ‚Üí No liability.

1.6 General Defenses and Discharge of Torts

Meaning
General Defences = Reasons that allow the defendant to escape liability even if a tort happened.

In simple words:
‚ÄúYes, harm happened, but I am not legally responsible because of this defence.‚Äù

1. Consent (Volenti Non Fit Injuria)
If the plaintiff agreed/accepted the risk, no liability.
Example:
You watch a cricket match ‚Üí ball hits you
You join boxing ‚Üí you may get hurt
‚Üí You accepted the risk ‚Üí no compensation

2. Act of God (Natural Forces)
Damage caused by natural events that humans cannot prevent.
Example:
Earthquake
Flood
Lightning
‚Üí No liability because no one controlled it.

3. Inevitable Accident
Accident that could not be prevented even with full care.
Example:
A sudden tire burst of a well-maintained car.
‚Üí Driver not liable.

4. Private Defence (Self-Defence)
A person can protect life, body, property with reasonable force.
Example:
A thief attacks B ‚Üí B pushes him in self-defence ‚Üí no liability.

5. Necessity
Doing harm to prevent bigger harm.
Example:
A cuts B‚Äôs land to stop a spreading fire ‚Üí no liability.

6. Statutory Authority
If law authorises an act, no liability.
Example:
Municipal corporation demolishes illegal construction ‚Üí cannot be sued.

7. Mistake (Sometimes accepted)
An honest mistake without negligence may be a defence.
Example:
Police arrest a person who looks like the real suspect ‚Üí genuine mistake ‚Üí no liability.

8. Plaintiff‚Äôs Own Wrong (Plaintiff at Fault)
If the injury happened due to plaintiff‚Äôs own carelessness, defendant not liable.
Example:
A ignores ‚ÄúDANGER ‚Äì HIGH VOLTAGE‚Äù sign and touches the wire.
‚Üí A at fault ‚Üí no claim.

9. Contributory Negligence
Both parties share the fault ‚Üí defendant‚Äôs liability reduces or ends.
Example:
Both drivers were careless ‚Üí damages divided.

3.1 Vicarious Liability: Basis, scope, Justification, Different types

3.1 Vicarious Liability
Meaning:
Vicarious liability = One person is held liable for the wrongful act of another, due to a special relationship.
Master liable for servant‚Äôs act
Employer liable for employee
Principal liable for agent

Basis of Vicarious Liability (Why it is applied?):
1. Master‚ÄìServant Relationship
If a servant (employee) does a wrongful act in the course of employment, the master (employer) is liable.
2. Control Test
The master has the right to control how the servant works ‚Üí
So master is liable.
3. Benefit Test
Employee was doing something that benefited the employer,
So employer must take responsibility.
4. Social Convenience
To make sure injured people get compensation from someone who can pay (employer).

3. Scope (When it applies):
Vicarious liability applies when:
‚úì The wrongdoer is an employee, not an independent contractor
Example: Driver hired on salary ‚Üí employer liable
Uber/Auto driver (independent) ‚Üí company not liable
‚úì The act was done during course of employment
Meaning:
During working hours
Using employer‚Äôs tools/vehicle
For employer‚Äôs work
Employer NOT liable if:
Employee was doing personal work
Employee acted outside employment
Example: Driver takes car for girlfriend ‚Üí accident ‚Üí employer not liable

4. Justification (Why law makes employer responsible?):
‚úîEmployer gets the benefit of employee‚Äôs work
So employer must take the risk too.
‚úî Employer has control over the employee
So employer must ensure safety and good conduct.
‚úî Victim can get compensation easily
Employer generally has more money or insurance.
‚úî Encourages employers to train employees properly

5. Different Types of Vicarious Liability

1. Master & Servant (Most Important)
Employer is liable for acts of the employee done during the course of employment.
2. Principal & Agent
Principal is liable for actions of agent done within authority.
Example: Real estate agent makes a contract ‚Üí principal is bound.
3. Partners in Partnership Firm
Every partner is liable for torts done by another partner in the course of business.
4. State / Government Liability
Government is liable for acts of public servants while performing government duties.
5. Parents & Children (in some cases)
Parents may be liable if the child was acting under their control (rare in India).
6. Company & Directors / Employees
Company is liable for mistakes committed by employees during official work.

3.2 Principle of Strict Liability: Rylands v. Fletcher (with exceptions)


Meaning of Strict Liability:
Strict Liability = A person is liable for the harm even if they were not negligent.
‚ÄúGalti chahe ho ya na ho, nuksaan hua to bharna padega‚Äù
If someone keeps something dangerous on their land and it escapes and causes damage ‚Üí owner must pay.

Rylands v. Fletcher (1868) ‚Äì Main Case:
Facts:
Rylands built a reservoir (water tank).
Water leaked through old mine shafts and flooded Fletcher‚Äôs coal mine.
Fletcher suffered loss.
Held:
Rylands was liable even though he did not know about the old shafts.
Strict Liability principle created.

Essential Conditions of Strict Liability:
  1. Dangerous thing brought on land
  2. Non-natural use of land
  3. Thing must escape
  4. Damage must be caused

Exceptions to Strict Liability:
  1. Act of God (natural disaster)
  2. Act of third party
  3. Consent of the plaintiff
  4. Statutory authority
  5. Plaintiff‚Äôs own fault
3. Essential Conditions (When strict liability applies?)
1. Dangerous Thing
Something harmful if it escapes:
Water, gas, explosives, electricity, chemicals, oil, wild animals.
2. Non-Natural Use of Land
means: üëâ Using land in an unusual or special way that increases danger to others, not the ordinary, everyday use of land. Use which increases danger.
Examples:
Storing large water tank
Chemical factory
Keeping explosives
Not natural use: gardening, ordinary water supply.
3. Escape of the Thing
The danger must leave the defendant‚Äôs property and cause damage.
Example: Water leaks out & floods neighbor‚Äôs land.

4. Exceptions to Strict Liability
1. Plaintiff‚Äôs Fault
If the injured person helped cause the harm, defendant not liable.
Example: Someone opens the gate of a water tank and water flows out.
2. Act of God (Natural Disaster)
Damage caused by uncontrollable natural forces.
Example: Earthquake breaks dam ‚Üí water escapes ‚Üí no liability.
3. Act of a Third Party
A stranger (not employee) causes the escape.
Example: Unknown person breaks the tank ‚Üí water escapes.
4. Consent of Plaintiff (Volenti Non Fit Injuria)
If the plaintiff agreed to the risk.
Example: Two neighbours share a water tank ‚Üí both consented to risk.
5. Statutory Authority
If the act is authorized by law and done carefully ‚Üí no liability.
Example: Government water tank bursts despite best precautions.
6. Natural Use of Land
If use of land is ordinary or natural ‚Üí strict liability doesn‚Äôt apply.
Example: Normal household water pipeline bursts.

3.3 Principle of Absolute Liability

Bhopal Gas Disaster case and Shree Ram Food Oleum Gas Leakage case and orientation to Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991


1. Bhopal Gas Disaster Case (1984)
Union Carbide Case ‚Äì Basis of Absolute Liability in India

What happened?
A poisonous gas Methyl Isocyanate (MIC) leaked from the Union Carbide pesticide plant in Bhopal.
Thousands died instantly; lakhs suffered permanent injuries.
Worst industrial disaster in the world.

Key Points:
Company argued: ‚ÄúIt was an accident, not our fault.‚Äù
Supreme Court: Company must pay compensation.
Government of India settled for $470 million.

Legal Principle Developed:
Absolute Liability
If an industry deals with ultra-hazardous (very dangerous) substances, and they escape ‚Üí
The company is 100% liable, even if:
They took all safety measures
It was an accident
Error by a third party
Act of God

No excuses allowed.
Stronger than Strict Liability.

2. Shree Ram Food & Fertilizer (Oleum Gas Leak Case, 1985)
Also known as M.C. Mehta vs Union of India

What happened?
Oleum gas leaked from Shree Ram factory in Delhi.
1 person died; many were injured.
Public interest litigation filed by M.C. Mehta, an environmental lawyer.
Court‚Äôs Important Rulings

1. Absolute Liability Introduced
Supreme Court said:
Industries dealing with dangerous chemicals have absolute liability.
No exceptions, no escape.
This became a foundation for environmental safety law in India.
2. ‚ÄúDeep Pocket Principle‚Äù
Big industries earning profits must also bear responsibility for safety.
3. Duty to Install Safety Systems
Gas industries must keep:
alarms
emergency shutdown
regular inspections
safety training

4. Laid groundwork for Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991

Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991
Purpose:
Created after Bhopal & Oleum gas accidents.
To ensure instant compensation to people affected by accidents involving hazardous substances.

Why created?
Earlier compensation took years (like Bhopal case).
People needed fast and direct payment.

Key Features of PLI Act, 1991
1. Mandatory Insurance
All industries handling dangerous substances must take insurance,
so victims get quick compensation.
2. No Need to Prove Negligence
Victim gets compensation without proving company was at fault.
Just show that accident happened ‚Üí compensation automatically applies.
3. Immediate Relief
Company + insurance company must give instant money for:
death
injury
property damage
4. Environmental Relief Fund (ERF)
A special fund made by Government,
helps when compensation is more than insurance amount.
5. Strict Punishment
If company operates without insurance ‚Üí heavy fines + closure.

3.4 Judicial and Extra Judicial Remedies


1. Meaning
Judicial Remedies
Remedies given by Court after the victim files a case.
Court helps you.

Extra-Judicial Remedies
Remedies taken by the victim without going to court (self-help but legally allowed).
You help yourself (legally).

2. Judicial Remedies (Court Remedies)
These are the remedies a court gives when a tort is committed.
1. Damages (Money Compensation)
The most common remedy.
Types (in easy language):
Nominal damages ‚Üí small amount; rights violated but no big loss.
Compensatory damages ‚Üí actual loss suffered.
Punitive / Exemplary damages ‚Üí to punish the wrongdoer.
Aggravated damages ‚Üí extra compensation for humiliation or mental pain.
2. Injunction
Court order to stop or prevent a wrongful act.
Types:
Prohibitory injunction ‚Üí Stop doing something.
Mandatory injunction ‚Üí Court orders you to do something.
Example: Stop loud noise; remove illegal construction.
3. Specific Restitution of Property
Court orders the return of wrongfully taken goods/property.
Example: If someone takes your bike illegally, court orders it returned.
4. Specific Performance
Court orders a party to perform a promised duty (usually in contracts).

3. Extra-Judicial Remedies (Self-Help Remedies)
These are actions a person can legally take without going to court.
Extra-Judicial Remedies (Self-Help Remedies)
1. Self-Defense (Private Defense)
You can protect yourself, your property, or someone else from harm.
But force used must be reasonable, not excessive.
2. Expulsion of Trespasser
You can remove a trespasser from your property peacefully.
Example: Someone enters your land ‚Üí you can tell them to leave or gently guide them out (without violence).
3. Re-entry on Land
If someone illegally occupies your land, you can peacefully re-enter your own land.
4. Re-caption / Re-possession of Goods
If someone wrongfully takes your goods, you can take them back immediately, without going to court.
Example: Your stolen bag is found with a thief ‚Üí you can take it back.
5. Abatement of Nuisance
You can remove the source of nuisance yourself.
Example:
Overhanging branch entering your property ‚Üí you can cut the part entering your side.
Loudspeaker causing noise ‚Üí you can switch it off if safe.
6. Distress Damage Feasant
If an animal enters your land and causes damage, you can seize the animal until the owner pays compensation.
Example: Neighbor‚Äôs cow destroys your crops ‚Üí you can hold the cow.

A central law that regulates:
  1. Road safety
  2. Licensing
  3. Registration
  4. Insurance
  5. Compensation for motor accident victims
Motor accident compensation is mainly dealt with under Sections 165 to 176.

2. What is a Motor Accident?
Any accident involving a motor vehicle resulting in:
  1. Death
  2. Bodily injury
Motor Accident Claims Damage to vehicle / property

3. Motor Accident Claim Type
Motor accident claims generally fall under three types:
  1. Compensation for Death: Given to family/legal heirs when a person dies in a road accident.
  2. Compensation for Injury: For medical expenses, disability, pain & suffering, loss of income.

  1. Injured person

  3. Owner of damaged property
  4. Insurer (in special cases)

In the Motor Accident Claims Tribunal (MACT) of:
  1. The area where the accident happened
  2. The area where claimant lives
  3. The area where the vehicle owner/insurer resides or carries business
6. Motor Accident Claims Tribunal (MACT)
  1. A special court created under Section 165
  2. Purpose: Provide speedy and simple compensation
  3. It replaces civil courts for motor accident cases (Only MACT handles these cases ‚Äî not normal civil courts)
7. Composition of MACT Chairperson: A District Judge or Additional District Judge appointed by the State Government.

4.6 Liability Without Fault and Third-Party Risks under Motor Vehicles Act, 1988



Liability Without Fault (No-Fault Liability)
(Section 140 & Section 161 of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1988)

Meaning (Easy Terms):
Compensation is given even if the driver was not negligent.
There is no need to prove fault, rashness, or negligence.
This provision ensures quick relief to victims.
Section 140 ‚Äì No-Fault Compensation
Fixed Compensation:
  1. ‚Çπ2,50,000 for death
  2. ‚Çπ1,25,000 for permanent disability

Features:
  1. No need to prove whose mistake it was
Immediate / Timely Relief
Compensation is given quickly to the victim.
Paid By:
Paid by the vehicle owner and insurer.
Additional Benefit:
Given in addition to other compensation under fault-based claims.

Payment Source:
Paid by the Central Government through the ‚ÄúSolatium Fund‚Äù.
Why Liability Without Fault Exists?
Because:
  1. Victims need urgent money
  2. Legal cases take a long time
  3. Sometimes the driver runs away
  4. It is hard to prove negligence in many accidents

Fault-Based Liability vs No-Fault Liability

Fault-Based Liability (Section 166)
  1. Must prove driver was negligent
  2. Compensation varies and is usually higher
  3. Takes longer time
  4. Detailed evidence is required

No-Fault Liability (Section 140)
  1. No need to prove negligence
  2. Compensation is fixed and lower
  3. Provides fast relief
  4. Simple and quick process

Third-Party Risks (Compulsory Insurance)
(Sections 146‚Äì150)
Meaning of ‚ÄúThird Party‚Äù:
Any person other than the driver or owner of the vehicle.
Meaning When a vehicle causes an accident, any person other than the owner or driver is called a third party. The risk or loss suffered by them is called third-party risk.

Examples:
  1. Pedestrian
  2. Passenger
  3. Driver of another vehicle
  4. Property of others

Compulsory Third-Party Insurance (TP Insurance)
  1. Every vehicle must have third-party insurance
  2. Driving without it is illegal
  3. Purpose is to protect innocent victims

Covers:
  1. Damage to third-party property
  2. Legal liabilities of the owner or driver

Does NOT Cover:
  1. Damage to owner‚Äôs own vehicle (requires own-damage insurance)
  2. Driver‚Äôs own injuries (requires Personal Accident Cover)

Why Third-Party Insurance is Important?
Because:
  1. Accident victims get guaranteed compensation
  2. Prevents vehicle owner from paying huge amounts personally
  3. Ensures social security on roads

Insurer‚Äôs Liability in Third-Party Claims
The insurance company must pay when:
  1. The accident involved the insured vehicle
  2. A third party suffered injury, death, or property loss
  3. The policy was valid on the date of accident

Insurer May Refuse Only If:
  1. Driver had no valid driving license
  2. Vehicle was used for an unauthorized purpose
  3. Policy was obtained by fraud

Note:
Even in these situations, courts often protect the victim first and may direct the insurer to pay compensation initially.

When Both Apply?
Victim Can Get:
  1. Immediate no-fault compensation (Section 140)
  2. Higher fault-based compensation (Section 166)

Both compensations are allowed under law.
