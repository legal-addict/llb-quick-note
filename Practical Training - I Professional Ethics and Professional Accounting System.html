1.1 Enrolment of Lawyers- Qualifications prescribed u/s 24 of the Advocates Act 1961and procedure

ENROLMENT OF LAWYERS

Meaning of Enrolment
Enrolment means registration of a law graduate as an Advocate with the State Bar Council, which gives the right to practise law in courts.
Example: A student who completes LLB and registers with Maharashtra State Bar Council becomes an enrolled advocate.

Qualifications prescribed under Section 24 of the Advocates Act, 1961

1. Citizenship
The person must be a citizen of India.
Foreign nationals may be enrolled only if Indian citizens are permitted to practise law in that country.

2. Age
The person must have completed 21 years of age.

3. Educational Qualification
The person must possess an LLB degree from a university established by law in India or from a foreign university recognized by the Bar Council of India.

4. Good Moral Character
The person should be of good moral character and must not be convicted of an offence involving moral turpitude.
Example: A person convicted for cheating cannot be enrolled as an advocate.

5. No Disqualification
A person is disqualified if he or she has been dismissed from government service for moral misconduct or convicted of a serious criminal offence.
Example: A government employee dismissed for corruption is not eligible for enrolment.

6. Other Conditions
The applicant must pay the prescribed enrolment fees and fulfill conditions laid down by the State Bar Council.
Example: Payment of enrolment fee to the State Bar Council is mandatory.


Procedure for Enrolment of Advocates

Step 1: Application
Application is submitted to the State Bar Council in the prescribed form.

Step 2: Documents Required
LLB degree certificate
Mark sheets
Proof of age
Character certificate
Passport size photographs
Enrolment fee receipt

Step 3: Verification
The State Bar Council verifies the documents and eligibility of the applicant.

Step 4: Enrolment Committee
The application is examined by the Enrolment Committee of the State Bar Council.

Step 5: Entry in State Roll
If approved, the name of the applicant is entered in the State Roll of Advocates.

Step 6: Certificate of Enrolment
After enrolment, a Certificate of Enrolment is issued to the advocate.

Step 7: All India Bar Examination (AIBE)
The advocate must pass the All India Bar Examination to practise law in courts.

Important Points
Section 24 deals with qualifications for enrolment.
Enrolment is done by the State Bar Council.
Right to practise is obtained after enrolment and passing AIBE.

1.2 Common Terminology: Solicitor, Advocate, Lawyer, Senior Counsel, Junior Counsel, Legal Practitioners, Barrister, AOR,Amicus Curie

1. Solicitor
A solicitor is a legal professional who mainly deals with legal documentation, legal advice, and client matters outside the court.
Example: Preparing contracts, wills, and legal notices for clients.

2. Advocate
An advocate is a person enrolled under the Advocates Act, 1961 and is entitled to practise law and argue cases before courts in India.
Example: An advocate arguing a criminal case in the Sessions Court.

3. Lawyer
A lawyer is a general term used for a person who has studied law and gives legal advice or represents clients in legal matters.
Example: A lawyer advising a client on property dispute.

4. Senior Counsel
Senior Counsel is a designation given by the Supreme Court or High Courts to experienced and distinguished advocates.
They are not allowed to file cases directly and must work through a junior advocate or AOR.
Example:In a Supreme Court case, a junior advocate files the case, and the Senior Counsel only argues before the judges.

5. Junior Counsel
Junior Counsel is an advocate who assists Senior Counsel in preparing and presenting cases.
Example: Drafting pleadings and researching case laws for Senior Advocate.

6. Legal Practitioner
Legal Practitioner is a broad term which includes advocates, lawyers, attorneys, and others authorized to practise law.
Example: Any person legally permitted to provide legal services.

7. Barrister
A barrister is a lawyer trained in England who specializes in courtroom advocacy and legal opinions.
In India, barristers can practise after enrolment with State Bar Council.
Example: A barrister arguing cases before High Court after enrolment in India.

8. Advocate-on-Record (AOR)
An Advocate-on-Record is an advocate entitled to file and act in cases before the Supreme Court of India.
Only an AOR can file petitions in the Supreme Court.
Example: Filing a writ petition in Supreme Court through an AOR.

9. Amicus Curiae
Amicus Curiae means “Friend of the Court”. It is a person appointed by the court to assist it in a case.
Amicus Curiae is not a party to the case and gives impartial assistance.
Example:In a public interest case, the court asks an independent lawyer to help the judge understand the law. That lawyer is Amicus Curiae.

Conclusion
These terms are commonly used in legal profession and help in understanding different roles of legal professionals.

1.3 Seven Lamps of Advocacy: Honesty, Courage, Industry, Wit, Eloquence, Judgment and Fellowship

(The Seven Lamps of Advocacy were given by Justice Edward Abbott Parry(he was British Judge and legal writer.). These are the essential qualities of a good advocate.)

1. Honesty
An advocate must always be truthful to the court and should not mislead the judge.
Example: A lawyer tells the court the true facts even if it weakens his client’s case.

2. Courage
An advocate must be fearless and ready to defend his client according to law.
Example: A lawyer argues against a powerful person without fear in court.

3. Industry
Industry means hard work, preparation, and continuous study by an advocate.
Example: A lawyer studies case laws and prepares arguments properly before the hearing.

4. Wit
Wit means presence of mind and ability to respond intelligently in court.
Example: A lawyer gives a quick and sensible reply when the judge asks an unexpected question.

5. Eloquence
Eloquence means the ability to speak clearly and persuasively (convincing manner)in court.
Example: A lawyer explains his arguments in simple language so that the judge easily understands.

6. Judgment
Judgment means the ability to take correct decisions at the right time in court.
Example: A lawyer decides not to ask unnecessary questions during cross-examination.

7. Fellowship
Fellowship means respect, cooperation, and good relations with judges and fellow advocates.
Example: A lawyer behaves politely with the opposite counsel and maintains court discipline.

Conclusion
The Seven Lamps of Advocacy guide advocates to maintain professional ethics and become successful lawyers.

1.4 The Bar Council of India and the State Bar Council- Constitution, Powers and Functions

Constitution of Bar Council of India
The Bar Council of India is a statutory body established under the Advocates Act, 1961.

It consists of the following members:
Chairman and Vice-Chairman elected by its members.
One member elected from each State Bar Council.
The Attorney General of India and the Solicitor General of India are ex-officio members.

Powers and Functions of Bar Council of India
To lay down standards of professional conduct and etiquette for advocates
(It means that the Bar Council of India makes rules about how advocates should behave in court and in their profession.)
Example: An advocate must speak respectfully to the judge and must not mislead the court.
To prescribe standards of legal education in consultation with universities.
To recognize universities whose law degrees qualify for enrolment.
To conduct the All India Bar Examination (AIBE).
To exercise disciplinary control over State Bar Councils.
To safeguard the rights, privileges, and interests of advocates.

State Bar Council

Constitution of State Bar Council
A State Bar Council is constituted under the Advocates Act, 1961 for each State.

It consists of the following members:
Chairman and Vice-Chairman elected by its members.
Members elected from among advocates on the State roll.
The Advocate General of the State is an ex-officio member.

Powers and Functions of State Bar Council
To enroll advocates and maintain the State Roll of Advocates.
To admit persons as advocates on the State roll.
To entertain and decide cases of misconduct against advocates.
To safeguard the rights and interests of advocates in the State.
To promote and support law reform and legal education.
To conduct welfare schemes for advocates.

Difference between Bar Council of India and State Bar Council
Bar Council of India is an apex body at national level.
State Bar Council functions at the State level.
BCI deals with legal education and national standards.
State Bar Council deals with enrolment and discipline of advocates.

Conclusion
The Bar Council of India and State Bar Councils play an important role in regulating the legal profession and maintaining standards of advocacy in India.

1.5 Privileges of a lawyer- Salient features of the Advocates Act, 1961

Privileges of a Lawyer

1. Unified Legal Profession
It means that the Advocates Act, 1961 recognizes only one class of legal professionals called advocates.
Earlier, there were different categories like pleaders, vakils, and attorneys.
After the Act, all legal practitioners are known as advocates.
Example: Whether a lawyer practices in District Court or High Court, he is called an advocate.

2. Right to Appear in Courts
An advocate has the right to appear, argue, and represent clients before courts and tribunals.
Example: A lawyer arguing a case before the High Court.

3. Right to be Heard
An advocate has the right to present arguments and be heard by the court.
Example: A lawyer presenting legal points before the judge.

4. Right to Professional Fees
An advocate has the right to charge reasonable professional fees for legal services.
Example: Charging fees for drafting a legal notice.

5. Protection for Professional Acts
An advocate is protected for acts done in good faith during professional duties.
Example: A lawyer is not personally liable for arguments made in court in good faith.

6. Right to Client Confidentiality
An advocate has the privilege to keep client information confidential.
Example: A lawyer does not disclose client secrets to others.

Salient Features of the Advocates Act, 1961

1. Unified Legal Profession
The Act provides for one class of legal practitioners called advocates.
Example: All legal practitioners are known as advocates under the Act.

2. Establishment of Bar Councils
The Act establishes the Bar Council of India and State Bar Councils.

3. Enrolment of Advocates
The Act lays down qualifications and procedure for enrolment of advocates.

4. Right to Practice
The Act gives advocates the right to practise throughout India.

5. Disciplinary Control
The Act provides disciplinary action against advocates for misconduct.
Example: Suspension of advocate for professional misconduct.

6. Regulation of Legal Education
The Act empowers BCI to regulate legal education in India.
Example: Prescribing standards for law colleges.

Conclusion
The Advocates Act, 1961 protects the privileges of advocates and ensures proper regulation of the legal profession in India.

1.6 Right to Practice

Meaning of Right to Practice
Right to Practice means the legal right of an advocate to practise law and appear before courts, tribunals, and authorities after enrolment.

Statutory Provision
The Right to Practice is provided under Section 30 of the Advocates Act, 1961.

Who Can Exercise the Right to Practice
Only an advocate who is enrolled with a State Bar Council and has passed the All India Bar Examination can practise law.
Example: A person who has completed LLB but is not enrolled cannot practise law.

Scope of Right to Practice
An advocate has the right to practise throughout the territory of India.
An advocate can practise before all courts, tribunals, and authorities legally authorized to take evidence.
Example: An advocate enrolled in Rajasthan can practise in Tamil Nadu courts.

Restrictions on Right to Practice
The right to practise is subject to rules made by the Bar Council of India and State Bar Councils.
It can be suspended in case of professional misconduct.
Example: An advocate suspended by Bar Council cannot appear in court during suspension period.

Importance of Right to Practice
It ensures that only qualified and enrolled advocates practise law.
It protects the dignity and discipline of the legal profession.

Conclusion
Right to Practice is a statutory right given to advocates under the Advocates Act, 1961, subject to professional ethics and discipline.

1.7 Designation as Senior Counsel: Indira Jaising v/s SC of India (SC 2017)

Meaning of Senior Counsel
Senior Counsel (Senior Advocate) is an experienced advocate designated by the Supreme Court or High Courts due to ability, standing, and experience in law.

Legal Provision
Designation(action of choosing someone to hold an office or post.) of Senior Counsel is provided under Section 16 of the Advocates Act, 1961.

Powers and Restrictions of Senior Counsel
A Senior Counsel mainly argues cases in court.
He or she cannot file cases or pleadings directly.
A Senior Counsel must work through a junior advocate or Advocate-on-Record.

Case Law: Indira Jaising v. Supreme Court of India (2017)

Facts of the Case
Indira Jaising, a senior advocate, challenged the system of designation of Senior Counsel.
She argued that the existing process was arbitrary( based on random choice), biased, and lacked transparency.

Issues Raised
Whether the process of designation of Senior Counsel was fair and transparent.
Whether clear guidelines were required for such designation.

Judgment
The Supreme Court held that the designation of Senior Counsel must be fair, transparent, and objective.
The Court framed new guidelines for designation of Senior Counsel.
A permanent committee was formed to assess candidates.
Marks-based evaluation system was introduced.

Importance of the Case
The judgment removed arbitrariness in designation of Senior Counsel.
It ensured equality, transparency, and merit-based selection.

Simple Example
Earlier, Senior Counsel were chosen without clear rules.
After this judgment, advocates are selected based on experience, judgments, publications, and interviews.

Conclusion
Indira Jaising v. Supreme Court of India (2017) made the designation of Senior Counsel transparent and based on merit, ensuring fairness in the legal profession.

2.1 Professional Ethics- Power of Bar Council of India, Meaning and Standards of professional conduct and etiquette

Meaning of Professional Ethics
Professional ethics are the moral rules and principles which guide the behaviour of advocates in their professional life.
These ethics ensure honesty, discipline, and dignity in the legal profession.

Power of Bar Council of India

1. Power to Frame Rules
The Bar Council of India has the power to make rules regarding professional conduct and etiquette (polite behaviour)of advocates.
Example: Framing rules on how advocates should behave in court.

2. Power to Set Ethical Standards
The Bar Council of India lays down standards of professional behaviour for advocates.
Example: Rules against misleading the court or client.

3. Power of Disciplinary Control
The Bar Council of India can exercise disciplinary control over State Bar Councils.
Example: Giving directions in cases of professional misconduct.

4. Power to Punish for Misconduct
Through State Bar Councils, the Bar Council of India can suspend or remove advocates for professional misconduct.
Example: Suspending an advocate for unethical behaviour.

Meaning of Standards of Professional Conduct and Etiquette
Standards of professional conduct and etiquette are rules which guide advocates on how to behave towards the court, clients, and other advocates.

Standards of Professional Conduct and Etiquette

1. Duty Towards the Court
An advocate must act with respect and honesty towards the court.
Example: Not using disrespectful language before the judge.

2. Duty Towards the Client
An advocate must protect the interest of the client and maintain confidentiality.
Example: Not revealing client’s secrets to others.

3. Duty Towards Opponent and Other Advocates
An advocate must behave politely and maintain professional courtesy.
Example: Not shouting or insulting the opposite counsel.

4. Duty Towards Profession
An advocate must maintain the dignity and honour of the legal profession.
Example: Not engaging in illegal or dishonest practices.

Conclusion
Professional ethics and standards of conduct ensure fairness, discipline, and respect in the legal profession and are essential for effective administration of justice.

2.2 Duties
A. Duty to the court, B. Duty to the client, C. Duty to the opponent, D. Duty to the profession, E.Duty to the colleagues, F.Duty to public,state and society

Advocates play an important role in the administration of justice. Their duties are governed by the Advocates Act, 1961 and Bar Council of India Rules.

A. ADV. DUTY TO THE COURT
• Advocate is an officer of the court.
• Must act with dignity and self-respect.
• Must show respect towards judges and court staff.
• Should not use improper or insulting language.
• Must not mislead the court with false facts or evidence.
• Should follow proper dress code prescribed by Bar Council.
• Must not influence the court by illegal means.
• Should not boycott court or participate in strikes.
• Must conduct cases with honesty and fairness.

B.ADV DUTY TO THE CLIENT
• Must accept brief unless there is a valid reason to refuse.
• Must maintain confidentiality of client’s information.
• Should act in the best interest of the client.
• Must give honest and proper legal advice.
• Should not charge excessive or unreasonable fees.
• Should not withdraw from case without reasonable cause and notice.
• Must keep client informed about progress of the case.
• Must avoid conflict of interest.

C. ADV DUTY TO THE OPPONENT
• Advocate must act fairly towards opponent.
• Should not take unfair advantage of the opponent.
• Must not communicate directly with the opposite party.
• Should avoid personal attacks or abusive language.
• Must respect rights of opponent’s advocate.
• Should not suppress material facts.

D. ADV DUTY TO THE PROFESSION
• Legal profession is a noble profession.
• Advocate must maintain honour and dignity of profession.
• Must not advertise or solicit work.
• Should not indulge in corrupt or unethical practices.
• Must strictly follow Bar Council rules.

E. ADV DUTY TO THE COLLEAGUES
• Must maintain cordial and respectful relations with colleagues.
• Should not encourage unethical competition.
• Must not make false allegations against fellow advocates.
• Should guide and help junior advocates.
• Must observe professional courtesy.

F.ADV DUTY TO PUBLIC, STATE AND SOCIETY
• Advocate is a social engineer.
• Must promote justice and rule of law.
• Should provide legal aid to poor and needy.
• Must not support illegal or anti-social activities.
• Must respect the Constitution and laws of India.
• Should work for social welfare and legal awareness.

CONCLUSION
An advocate must balance duties towards the court, client, profession, colleagues and society to ensure justice and maintain dignity of the legal system.

2.3 Duty to Render Legal Aid

Legal aid means providing free legal services to poor, needy and disadvantaged persons who are unable to afford legal representation.

The duty to render legal aid is recognized under:
• Article 39A of the Constitution of India
• Advocates Act, 1961
• Bar Council of India Rules

MEANING OF LEGAL AID
Legal aid includes legal advice, drafting of documents, and representation before courts or tribunals without charging fees.

DUTY OF ADVOCATE TO RENDER LEGAL AID
• Advocate has a social and moral obligation to provide legal aid.
• Must not refuse legal aid work without reasonable cause.
• Should assist poor, helpless and marginalized persons.
• Must give honest and sincere service even in free cases.
• Should not treat legal aid cases as inferior or secondary.
• Must maintain professional ethics while rendering legal aid.

LEGAL AID AUTHORITIES
• National Legal Services Authority (NALSA)
• State Legal Services Authority
• District Legal Services Authority
• Taluk Legal Services Committee

PERSONS ENTITLED TO LEGAL AID
• Women and children
• Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes
• Victims of trafficking and beggars
• Persons with disability
• Persons in custody or jail
• Persons with low income

IMPORTANCE OF LEGAL AID
• Ensures access to justice for all.
• Promotes equality before law.
• Helps in protecting fundamental rights.
• Strengthens rule of law and democracy.

CONCLUSION
Rendering legal aid is not charity but a constitutional duty. An advocate must actively participate in legal aid services to ensure justice reaches every section of society.

3.1 Professional Misconduct

Professional misconduct refers to any act or omission by an advocate which violates the standards of professional conduct and etiquette prescribed under the Advocates Act, 1961 and Bar Council of India Rules.

MEANING OF PROFESSIONAL MISCONDUCT
Professional misconduct means behaviour which is unethical, improper, illegal or dishonourable and is unworthy of a member of the legal profession.

LEGAL PROVISIONS
• Section 35 – Punishment for professional or other misconduct (Advocates Act, 1961)
• Bar Council of India Rules

ACTS AMOUNTING TO PROFESSIONAL MISCONDUCT
• Misleading the court or suppressing material facts.
• Using false evidence or coaching witnesses.
• Charging excessive or illegal fees.
• Misappropriation of client’s money or property.
• Advertising or soliciting professional work.
• Showing disrespect to the court.
• Breach of client confidentiality.
• Abandoning case without sufficient cause.
• Engaging in corrupt or immoral practices.

PROCEDURE FOR DISCIPLINARY ACTION
• Complaint may be made to State Bar Council.
• Disciplinary Committee conducts inquiry.
• Advocate is given opportunity of being heard.
• Decision is passed after proper inquiry.

IMPORTANT CASE LAWS
• Noratanmal Chouraria v. M.R. Murli (2004) – Advocate must maintain high standards of ethics.
• In Re: Vinay Chandra Mishra (1995) – Misconduct includes contemptuous behaviour towards court.

CONCLUSION
Professional misconduct undermines the dignity of the legal profession. Advocates must strictly follow ethical standards to maintain public confidence in the administration of justice.

3.2 Punishment for misconduct

Punishment for professional misconduct is imposed on an advocate to maintain discipline, dignity and ethics of the legal profession.

The provisions relating to punishment are contained under Section 35 of the Advocates Act, 1961.

MEANING OF PUNISHMENT FOR MISCONDUCT
Punishment means disciplinary action taken against an advocate who is found guilty of professional or other misconduct.

DISCIPLINARY AUTHORITY
• State Bar Council – Primary authority to inquire into misconduct.
• Disciplinary Committee of the State Bar Council conducts inquiry.

TYPES OF PUNISHMENTS
Under Section 35(3), the Disciplinary Committee may pass the following orders:

• Dismissal of Complaint
– When misconduct is not proved.

• Reprimand
– Formal warning or expression of disapproval.

• Suspension from Practice
– Advocate is prohibited from practicing for a specified period.

• Removal of Name from State Roll
– Advocate is permanently debarred(prohibit) from legal practice.

APPEAL AGAINST PUNISHMENT
• Appeal lies to the Bar Council of India under Section 37.
• Further appeal lies to the Supreme Court of India under Section 38.

OBJECT OF PUNISHMENT
• To maintain professional discipline.
• To protect the interest of clients.
• To preserve the honour of legal profession.
• To ensure public confidence in justice system.

IMPORTANT CASE LAW
• In Re: Vinay Chandra Mishra (1995) – Advocate suspended for professional misconduct.

CONCLUSION
Punishment for misconduct acts as a deterrent(discourage) against unethical conduct. Strict disciplinary action ensures integrity and respect for the legal profession.

3.3 Remedies against the order of punishment

Remedies against the order of punishment refer to the legal remedies available to an advocate who is aggrieved by the disciplinary order passed for professional misconduct.

These remedies are provided under the Advocates Act, 1961 to ensure fairness and justice in disciplinary proceedings.

REMEDIES AVAILABLE

1. Appeal to the Bar Council of India
• Provided under Section 37 of the Advocates Act, 1961.
• Appeal lies against the order of State Bar Council.
• Appeal must be filed within 60 days from the date of order.
• Bar Council of India may confirm, modify or set aside the order.
Example:
If a State Bar Council suspends an advocate for one year for misconduct, the advocate can file an appeal before the Bar Council of India challenging the suspension.

2. Appeal to the Supreme Court of India
• Provided under Section 38 of the Advocates Act, 1961.
• Appeal lies against the order of the Bar Council of India.
• Supreme Court has final authority in disciplinary matters.
• Court may pass any order it deems just and proper.
Example:
If the Bar Council of India confirms the removal of name of an advocate from the State Roll, the advocate may file an appeal before the Supreme Court.

3. Review or Reconsideration
• Disciplinary authority may reconsider the matter if there is error apparent on the face of record.
• Helps in correcting procedural or factual mistakes.
Example:
If punishment is passed without giving opportunity of hearing, the advocate can seek reconsideration of the order on the ground of violation of natural justice.

OBJECT OF PROVIDING REMEDIES
• To ensure natural justice.
• To prevent arbitrary or unfair punishment.
• To protect the rights of advocates.
• To maintain confidence in disciplinary system.

IMPORTANT CASE LAW
• Bar Council of India v. Aparna Basu – Right of appeal is an important safeguard in disciplinary proceedings.

CONCLUSION
The remedies against the order of punishment ensure that disciplinary powers are exercised judiciously. Appeal provisions uphold fairness and protect the dignity of the legal profession.

3.4 Review by the State Bar Council of its own order

Review by the State Bar Council means the power of the State Bar Council to reconsider, modify or correct its own disciplinary order in certain circumstances.

This power is exercised to ensure justice, fairness and correction of errors.

MEANING OF REVIEW
Review is a process by which the State Bar Council examines its own order to rectify any mistake, error or violation of principles of natural justice.

GROUNDS FOR REVIEW
Review may be allowed on the following grounds:
• Error apparent on the face of record.
• Violation of principles of natural justice (no hearing given).
• Procedural irregularity during inquiry.
• Discovery of new and important evidence.

POWER OF STATE BAR COUNCIL
• State Bar Council has inherent power to review its own order.
• Power must be exercised judiciously and sparingly.
• Review cannot be used as a substitute for appeal.

EXAMPLE
If an advocate is suspended without giving him an opportunity of being heard, the State Bar Council may review its order and set aside or modify the punishment.

LIMITATIONS ON REVIEW
• Review cannot be done arbitrarily.
• It cannot be exercised once the matter is pending in appeal.
• Review is not meant for re-hearing the entire case.

IMPORTANCE OF REVIEW POWER
• Ensures fairness and justice.
• Corrects errors and injustice.
• Upholds natural justice.
• Strengthens disciplinary mechanism.

CONCLUSION
The power of review enables the State Bar Council to correct its own mistakes and ensure justice. However, such power must be exercised cautiously to maintain discipline and credibility of the legal profession.

3.5 Disciplinary Committee of the State Bar Council- Organisation and powers and procedure

The Disciplinary Committee of the State Bar Council is the authority responsible for inquiring into complaints of professional misconduct against advocates under the Advocates Act, 1961.

LEGAL PROVISION
• Section 9 – Constitution of Disciplinary Committee
• Section 35 – Punishment for professional or other misconduct

ORGANISATION OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Constituted under Section 9 of the Advocates Act, 1961.
• Consists of three members elected by the State Bar Council.
• Two members shall be advocates on the State Roll.
• One member shall be a person qualified to be a Judge of a High Court.
• Senior-most member acts as the Chairman of the Committee.

POWERS OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Power to inquire into complaints of professional misconduct.
• Power to summon witnesses and examine evidence.
• Power to call for documents and records.
• Power to give opportunity of being heard to the advocate.
• Power to pass orders under Section 35 such as reprimand, suspension or removal from State Roll.

PROCEDURE FOLLOWED BY DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Complaint is filed before the State Bar Council.
• Matter is referred to the Disciplinary Committee.
• Notice is issued to the concerned advocate.
• Advocate is given reasonable opportunity of hearing.
• Evidence and documents are examined.
• Committee conducts a proper inquiry.
• Order is passed after following principles of natural justice.

EXAMPLE
If a complaint is made that an advocate has misappropriated client’s money, the State Bar Council refers the matter to its Disciplinary Committee, which conducts inquiry and may suspend or remove the advocate from practice if misconduct is proved.

IMPORTANCE OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Maintains professional discipline.
• Protects interest of clients.
• Preserves dignity of legal profession.
• Ensures ethical conduct among advocates.

CONCLUSION
The Disciplinary Committee of the State Bar Council plays a vital role in maintaining professional standards. Its organisation, powers and procedure ensure that disciplinary action is taken fairly and effectively.

3.6 Disciplinary Committee of the Bar Council of India- Organisation and powers and procedure

The Disciplinary Committee of the Bar Council of India is the highest disciplinary authority under the Advocates Act, 1961, which deals with professional misconduct of advocates at the national level.

LEGAL PROVISIONS
• Section 9 – Constitution of Disciplinary Committee
• Section 36 – Disciplinary powers of Bar Council of India
• Section 36B – Transfer of proceedings to BCI

ORGANISATION OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Constituted under Section 9 of the Advocates Act, 1961.
• Consists of three members elected by the Bar Council of India.
• Two members shall be advocates enrolled on State Rolls.
• One member shall be a person qualified to be a Judge of the Supreme Court or High Court.
• Senior-most member acts as the Chairman of the Committee.

POWERS OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Power to entertain appeals against orders of State Bar Councils.
• Power to inquire into professional misconduct cases transferred to it.
• Power to summon witnesses and examine evidence.
• Power to confirm, modify or set aside disciplinary orders.
• Power to impose punishments under Section 35.

PROCEDURE FOLLOWED BY DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE
• Appeal or case is placed before the Bar Council of India.
• Matter is referred to its Disciplinary Committee.
• Notice is issued to the concerned advocate.
• Advocate is given reasonable opportunity of hearing.
• Committee examines records, evidence and submissions.
• Inquiry is conducted following principles of natural justice.
• Final order is passed in writing with reasons recorded.

EXAMPLE
If a State Bar Council suspends an advocate for misconduct and the advocate files an appeal under Section 37, the matter is heard by the Disciplinary Committee of the Bar Council of India, which may reduce, confirm or cancel the punishment.

IMPORTANCE OF DISCIPLINARY COMMITTEE OF BCI
• Ensures uniform disciplinary standards across India.
• Acts as a national supervisory authority.
• Protects the dignity of legal profession.
• Strengthens ethical legal practice.

CONCLUSION
The Disciplinary Committee of the Bar Council of India plays a crucial role in maintaining discipline and professional ethics. Its organisation, powers and procedure ensure fair and effective control over professional misconduct at the national level.

3.7 Transfer of proceedings from the State Bar Council to the Bar Council of India

Transfer of proceedings means shifting a disciplinary case of professional misconduct from the State Bar Council to the Bar Council of India for proper disposal.

This provision ensures speedy justice, fairness and uniformity in disciplinary proceedings under the Advocates Act, 1961.

LEGAL PROVISION
• Section 36B of the Advocates Act, 1961

GROUNDS FOR TRANSFER OF PROCEEDINGS
Proceedings may be transferred in the following situations:
• If the State Bar Council fails to dispose of the complaint within one year from the date of receipt.
• If delay in inquiry affects the interest of justice.
• To ensure uniform disciplinary standards across States.

POWER OF BAR COUNCIL OF INDIA
• Bar Council of India may withdraw proceedings from a State Bar Council.
• It may either dispose of the case itself or transfer it to its Disciplinary Committee.
• Bar Council of India has same powers as the State Bar Council in such matters.

PROCEDURE OF TRANSFER
• Complaint is filed before the State Bar Council.
• If not disposed within one year, proceedings stand transferred.
• Records are sent to the Bar Council of India.
• Case is placed before the Disciplinary Committee of BCI.
• Inquiry is continued following principles of natural justice.

EXAMPLE
If a complaint of professional misconduct against an advocate is pending before a State Bar Council for more than one year without final decision, the proceedings are automatically transferred to the Bar Council of India, which then conducts the inquiry and passes final orders.

IMPORTANCE OF TRANSFER PROVISION
• Prevents unnecessary delay in disciplinary cases.
• Ensures speedy disposal of complaints.
• Maintains disciplinary control at national level.
• Protects interest of advocates and complainants.

CONCLUSION
Transfer of proceedings under Section 36B acts as a safeguard against delay and injustice. It empowers the Bar Council of India to ensure that disciplinary matters are resolved fairly and efficiently.

4.1 The Contempt Law and Practice- Articles 129 and 215 of the Constitution of India, Contempt of Courts Act, 1971

Contempt of court refers to any act which disrespects, obstructs, or interferes with the administration of justice or authority of the court.

LEGAL PROVISIONS
1. Articles 129 & 215 of the Constitution of India
• Article 129: Supreme Court has power to punish for contempt of itself.
• Article 215: High Courts have power to punish for contempt of themselves.

2. Contempt of Courts Act, 1971
• Defines the law relating to contempt of courts in India.
• Provides procedure for punishment and trial of contempt cases.
• Divides contempt into two types:
   • Criminal Contempt – Acts which scandalize or lower authority of court or interfere with justice.
   • Civil Contempt – Willful disobedience of court orders or injunctions.

CONTEMPT OF COURT – MEANING
• Any act or conduct which disrespects the court or obstructs justice.
• Aimed at protecting authority, dignity, and independence of judiciary.

EXAMPLES
• Publishing a newspaper article that scandals or ridicules a court judgment → Criminal contempt.
• Willfully refusing to follow a court order (like not paying compensation) → Civil contempt.

POWERS OF COURT
• Supreme Court & High Courts can initiate contempt proceedings suo moto or on petition.
• Can impose fine or imprisonment for contempt.
• Proceedings must follow natural justice principles.

OBJECTIVES OF CONTEMPT LAW
• Maintain authority and dignity of courts.
• Ensure fair and smooth administration of justice.
• Protect public confidence in judiciary.

CONCLUSION
Contempt law is essential to preserve judicial authority. It balances freedom of speech with the need to protect courts from disrespect and obstruction of justice.

4.2 Bar Bench relations - Role of the Bar to Strengthen Bar-Bench Relations

Bar-Bench relations refer to the mutual relationship between the advocates (Bar) and the judges (Bench) that ensures efficient administration of justice.

IMPORTANCE OF BAR-BENCH RELATIONS
• Maintains dignity and respect for courts and advocates.
• Promotes smooth and fair conduct of court proceedings.
• Helps in timely justice delivery.
• Strengthens public confidence in the judicial system.

ROLE OF THE BAR TO STRENGTHEN BAR-BENCH RELATIONS
1. Professionalism: Advocates must maintain high standards of ethics, honesty, and decorum (respectable behavior)in court.
2. Respect to Judges: Lawyers must address judges politely and avoid rude or contemptuous behavior.
3. Cooperation: Bar should cooperate with the Bench for efficient case management.
4. Avoid Unnecessary Adjournments: Minimizes delay and shows respect for court time.
5. Professional Conduct Outside Court: Avoid public criticism or media statements against judges which may scandalize the court.
6. Promote Legal Awareness: Educate junior advocates about ethics and proper courtroom behavior.
7. Active Participation: Bar should actively participate in law reform, seminars, and discussions to maintain a positive image.

EXAMPLE:
If advocates always follow court decorum, avoid arguing rudely, and respect deadlines, it reduces friction with the Bench and ensures timely disposal of cases.

CONCLUSION
Good Bar-Bench relations depend on mutual respect, professionalism, and cooperation. The Bar plays a vital role in strengthening this relationship by maintaining ethics, decorum, and cooperation with the Bench.

4.3 Accountancy for Lawyers
i.Amount due to the client or amount due by the client;
ii.Penalty for not keeping Account Books;
iii.Bar council Rules relating to accounting.
iv.Basic principles of accounting, financial statements, balance sheet, income statement

Accountancy is essential for lawyers to maintain proper financial records, ensure transparency, and comply with Bar Council Rules.

I. AMOUNT DUE TO THE CLIENT OR AMOUNT DUE BY THE CLIENT

• Amount due to the client: Money received by the advocate on behalf of the client, such as fees held in trust or settlement amounts, which must be paid to the client promptly.
• Amount due by the client: Fees, expenses, or disbursements payable by the client to the advocate for professional services rendered.
• Proper ledger accounts must be maintained to track both.
Example: Lawyer receives Rs. 50,000 for court fees. His professional fee is Rs. 5,000. Remaining Rs. 45,000 is due to the client.

II. PENALTY FOR NOT KEEPING ACCOUNT BOOKS
• Advocates are required to maintain proper books of accounts of all transactions.
• Failure to maintain accounts may result in:
   • Disciplinary action by State Bar Council.
   • Fine or reprimand under Bar Council rules.
• Ensures transparency and trust between advocate and client.
Example: Advocate does not maintain records of client money. Bar Council inspects accounts and imposes a fine and warning.

III. BAR COUNCIL RULES RELATING TO ACCOUNTING
• Advocates must keep:
   • Cash book and ledger accounts for receipts and payments.
   • Accounts showing fees received, expenses, and money held for clients.
• Records must be preserved for minimum 3 years.
• Bar Council may inspect accounts to ensure compliance.
Example: Lawyer maintains cash book showing Rs. 1,00,000 received from clients, Rs. 10,000 office expenses, Rs. 90,000 payable to clients. Bar Council verifies accounts.

IV. BASIC PRINCIPLES OF ACCOUNTING
• Double-entry system: Every transaction affects two accounts – debit and credit.
• Accrual principle: Income and expenses are recorded when earned or incurred, not when received or paid.
• Consistency principle: Same accounting methods must be used regularly.
• Prudence principle: Anticipate losses but not gains.
Example: Lawyer provides services in December but receives payment in January. Record income in December (accrual principle).

FINANCIAL STATEMENTS FOR LAWYERS
1. Balance Sheet: Shows financial position at a point in time.
• Assets = Money, property, receivables.
• Liabilities = Money owed to clients, creditors.
• Owner’s Equity = Capital + retained earnings.
Example: Assets = Rs. 2,00,000 (bank + cash), Liabilities = Rs. 1,50,000 (client money), Equity = Rs. 50,000

2. Income Statement / Profit & Loss Account: Shows financial performance over a period.
• Income = Fees earned, interest received.
• Expenses = Rent, salaries, court fees, office expenses.
• Net Profit / Loss = Income – Expenses.
Example: Income = Rs. 1,00,000, Expenses = Rs. 70,000, Net Profit = Rs. 30,000

CONCLUSION
Maintaining proper accounts ensures financial transparency, protects clients’ money, and keeps the advocate compliant with Bar Council rules. It also helps lawyers track profits, liabilities, and financial health of their practice.
