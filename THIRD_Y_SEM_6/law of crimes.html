1.1 History of Criminal law Difference between civil and criminal law

History of Criminal Law

Criminal law deals with offences committed against society and the State.
Its main object is to maintain law and order and punish wrongdoers.

Ancient Period
In ancient times there were no written criminal laws.
Punishments were based on customs, religion and revenge system.
The principle of “eye for an eye” was followed.

Hindu Law Period
Criminal law was found in Manusmriti.
Punishment depended upon caste and social status.
King was the supreme authority to administer justice.

Muslim Law Period
Criminal law was based on Quran and Shariat.
Crimes were classified as Hadd, Qisas and Tazir.
Justice was based on morality and religion.

British Period
Before British rule there was no uniform criminal law in India.
In 1860 Indian Penal Code was enacted.
It was drafted by Lord Macaulay.
IPC provided uniform definitions and punishments for crimes.

Modern Criminal Law
Indian Penal Code governed criminal law for many years.
Now it is replaced by Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023.
Modern criminal law focuses on human rights and fair trial.

Difference Between Civil Law and Criminal Law

Civil Law
Civil law deals with private disputes between individuals.
Its main object is to provide compensation or relief.
Case is filed by the aggrieved person.
Standard of proof is preponderance (fact of being greater in number)of probabilities.
Example: breach of contract, property dispute.

Criminal Law
Criminal law deals with offences against society and State.
Its main object is to punish the offender.
Case is prosecuted by the State.
Standard of proof is beyond reasonable doubt.
Example: theft, murder, cheating.

Conclusion
Civil law compensates the victim.
Criminal law punishes the wrongdoer.
Criminal law is stricter in nature to protect society.

1.2 Concept of Crime, Components of Crime and Criminal Liability

Concept of Crime
A crime is an act or illegal omission (ailure to fulfil a moral or legal obligation.) punishable by law.
A crime is considered a wrong against society and the State.
It creates public harm and disturbs social order.
In criminal law, intention or knowledge of the act is important.
Example
If a person steals another person’s mobile phone, it is a crime because it is prohibited by law and punishable by the State.

Definition of Crime
Crime means any act or omission which is prohibited by law and punishable by the State.
Example: theft, murder, cheating, assault.

Components of Crime
To constitute a crime, the following two essential elements must be present.

1. Actus Reus
Actus Reus means the wrongful or prohibited act or omission.
It is the physical conduct of the accused.
A mere intention without action is not punishable.
Example: taking another person’s property dishonestly.

2. Mens Rea
Mens Rea means a guilty mind or criminal intention.
It shows the mental element behind the act.
Mens Rea includes intention, knowledge, recklessness or negligence.
Without Mens Rea, an act does not usually become a crime.
Example
If a person steals a wallet knowing that it belongs to someone else, the guilty intention to steal is Mens Rea.

Actus Reus:commit wrongful act
Mens Rea: act with intention
if one of them is missing it'll not call crime
Example
If a person plans to steal but does not commit the act, there is no crime.
If a person accidentally takes another person’s bag believing it to be his own, there is no Mens Rea, so no crime.

Exceptions to Mens Rea
In certain offences Mens Rea (intention) is not required.
These are known as strict liability offences.
Example: traffic offences, food adulteration cases.

Criminal Liability
Criminal liability means legal responsibility for committing a crime.
A person is criminally liable when both Actus Reus(commit) and Mens Rea (intention) are present.
Punishment is imposed by the court for criminal liability.

Basis of Criminal Liability
The basis of criminal liability is the concurrence of guilty act and guilty mind.
Both must exist at the same time.

Exceptions to Criminal Liability
Criminal liability may not arise in cases of insanity (irrationality).
Acts done under mistake of fact are exempted (free from an obligation).
Acts done by children below the age of criminal responsibility are exempted.
Acts done in private defence are also exceptions.

Conclusion
Crime consists of both wrongful act and guilty intention.
Criminal liability arises only when law recognizes the offence.
The aim of criminal law is to punish the offender and protect society.

1.3 Theories and Kinds of Punishment
(Includes IPC Chapter III: Sections 53 to 75)

Meaning of Punishment
Punishment means penalty imposed by the court on a person who commits a crime.
The main purpose of punishment is to protect society and prevent crimes.

Theories of Punishment

1. Deterrent Theory
This theory aims to discourage the offender and others from committing crimes.
Punishment is given to create fear in the minds of people.
Example
Punishing a thief with imprisonment discourages others from stealing.

2. Preventive Theory
This theory prevents the offender from committing further crimes.
The offender is disabled from repeating the offence.
Example
Keeping a habitual offender in prison prevents him from committing more crimes.

3. Retributive Theory
This theory is based on the principle of revenge or retaliation.
The offender is punished because he has committed a wrong.
Example
Punishing a murderer because he has caused harm to society.

4. Reformative Theory
This theory focuses on reforming the offender rather than punishing him harshly.
It believes that criminals can be changed into good citizens.
Example
Providing education and training to prisoners to reform their behaviour.

5. Expiatory Theory
This theory believes punishment cleanses the guilt of the offender.
It is based on moral repentance and atonement.
Example
A person feeling guilt and reforming himself after punishment.

Kinds of Punishment under IPC (Section 53)

1. Death Penalty
Death penalty is the severest form of punishment.
It is awarded in the rarest of rare cases.
Example
Death penalty for heinous offences like brutal murder or terrorism cases.

2. Imprisonment for Life
Life imprisonment means imprisonment for the remainder of the convict’s natural life.
Example
Life imprisonment for murder under IPC.

3. Imprisonment
Imprisonment may be rigorous or simple.
Rigorous imprisonment involves hard labour.
Simple imprisonment does not involve hard labour.
Example
Rigorous imprisonment for robbery and simple imprisonment for minor offences.

4. Forfeiture of Property
Property of the offender may be confiscated (seize) by the State.
Example
Forfeiture of property of a person convicted of serious offences against the State.

5. Fine
Fine is a monetary penalty imposed by the court.
It may be imposed alone or along with imprisonment.
Example
Fine imposed for traffic offences or minor criminal acts.

Other Provisions Related to Punishment (Sections 54 to 75)
Commutation of sentence allows reduction of punishment.
Solitary (isolating) confinement may be awarded under specific conditions.
Enhanced punishment may be imposed for repeat offenders.
Example
Reducing death sentence to life imprisonment under commutation powers.
Higher punishment for a person repeatedly committing the same offence.

Conclusion
Punishment plays an important role in criminal justice system.
Different theories aim at deterrence, prevention and reformation.
IPC provides various kinds of punishments to maintain law and order.

1.4 Capital Punishment

Meaning of Capital Punishment
Capital punishment means punishment of death awarded by a court of law.
It is the most severe form of punishment in criminal law.
It is imposed only for the gravest (serious) offences.

Purpose of Capital Punishment
The purpose is to deter (discourage ) serious crimes and protect society.
It is also based on retribution for heinous offences.

Capital Punishment under Indian Law
In India, capital punishment is permitted by law.
It is awarded only in the rarest of rare cases.
Courts must give special reasons for awarding death penalty.

Rarest of Rare Doctrine
The death penalty is imposed only when life imprisonment is not sufficient.
The court considers the nature of crime and the criminal.

Example
Brutal murder involving extreme cruelty may fall under the rarest of rare cases.

Offences Punishable with Death
Certain offences provide death penalty as punishment.
These include murder and offences against the State.

Example
Murder involving brutal violence or terrorism-related offences.

Arguments in Favour of Capital Punishment
It acts as a strong deterrent against serious crimes.
It delivers justice to victims and society.
It permanently prevents the offender from repeating the crime.

Arguments Against Capital Punishment
There is a possibility of judicial error.
It violates the right to life and human dignity.
It does not give a chance for reform of the offender.

Procedure and Safeguards
Death sentence must be confirmed by the High Court.
The accused has the right to appeal and seek mercy petition.

Example
A convict may file a mercy petition before the President of India.

Current Position
Capital punishment exists but is rarely awarded.
Indian courts follow strict guidelines while awarding death penalty.

Conclusion
Capital punishment is an exceptional punishment.
It is imposed only in the rarest of rare cases.
The law aims to balance justice, deterrence and human rights.

1.5 Stages of Crime

Meaning of Stages of Crime
A crime is not committed suddenly.
It passes through different stages before completion.
Each stage shows the progress of criminal intention into action.

Stages of Crime
There are four main stages of crime.

1. Intention
Intention is the first stage of crime.
It means the mental decision to commit a crime.
Intention alone is not punishable under criminal law.
Example
A person decides in his mind to steal a bicycle but does nothing further.

2. Preparation
Preparation means arranging the means to commit the crime.
Generally, preparation is not punishable.
In some serious offences, preparation itself is punishable.
Example
A person buys a knife to commit murder.

Exceptions where Preparation is Punishable
Preparation to wage war against the State is punishable.
Preparation to commit dacoity (violent) is punishable.

3. Attempt
Attempt is a direct movement towards the commission of the crime.
The offender has done something more than preparation.
Attempt is punishable even if the crime is not completed.
Example
A person tries to pick another person’s pocket but fails.

4. Commission or Completion
This is the final stage where the crime is completed.
The intended offence is fully carried out.
Example
A person successfully steals the bicycle.

Importance of Stages of Crime
They help in determining criminal liability.
They help courts decide the degree of punishment.

Conclusion
Every crime passes through intention, preparation, attempt and completion.
Punishment depends upon the stage at which the crime is stopped.
Criminal law aims to prevent crimes at the earliest possible stage.

1.6 Theories of Negligence

Meaning of Negligence
Negligence means failure to take reasonable care which a prudent person would take.
It results in harm or injury to another person.
Negligence may arise due to carelessness or lack of attention.
Example
If a driver drives carelessly and hits a pedestrian, it amounts to negligence.

Theories of Negligence

1. Subjective Theory of Negligence
This theory considers the personal capacity of the accused.
The conduct of the accused is judged according to his own ability and understanding.
It focuses on the mental condition of the person.
Example
A child causing harm is judged differently from an adult because of lesser understanding.

2. Objective Theory of Negligence
This theory judges negligence based on the conduct of a reasonable person.
Personal capacity of the accused is not considered.
It applies a uniform standard of care for everyone.
Example:
If a driver breaks traffic rules, he is negligent even if he says he did not know the rule.

3. Reasonable Man Theory
According to this theory, negligence is determined by comparing conduct with that of a reasonable man.
A reasonable man is a cautious and prudent person.
Example
A reasonable person would stop at a red signal.
Failure to stop shows negligence.

4. Gross Negligence Theory
Gross negligence refers to extreme carelessness.
It shows total disregard for the safety of others.
Criminal liability generally arises in cases of gross negligence.
Example
A doctor performing surgery while intoxicated is grossly negligent.

Negligence in Criminal Law
In criminal law, negligence must be of a high degree to attract punishment.
Simple negligence is usually not punishable.
Example
A doctor making a minor mistake is not criminally liable.
But a doctor ignoring basic medical procedures may be criminally liable.

Conclusion
Negligence is failure to take due care.
Theories of negligence help courts decide liability.
Criminal negligence requires gross or serious negligence.

1.7 Introduction to Cyber Crimes

1.7 Introduction to Cyber Crimes in Criminal Law

Meaning of Cyber Crime
Cyber crime refers to illegal activities committed using computers, internet, or digital devices.
These crimes are done in cyberspace and affect individuals, organizations, or governments.

Nature of Cyber Crimes
Cyber crimes are modern crimes that use technology as a tool or target.
They can be committed from any place in the world due to the internet.

Examples of Cyber Crimes
Hacking – Unauthorized access to computer systems.
Online fraud – Cheating people through fake emails or websites.
Identity theft – Stealing personal information of others.
Cyber stalking – Harassing someone through online platforms.

Importance in Criminal Law
Cyber crimes threaten privacy, security, and property of people.
Criminal law includes cyber crimes to protect society from digital threats.
In India, cyber crimes are mainly dealt with under the Information Technology Act, 2000 along with IPC provisions.

Conclusion
With the increase in use of technology, cyber crimes are also increasing.
Therefore, understanding cyber crimes is essential in criminal law.

Extent and Operation -
IPC Chapter I: Sections 1 – 5

Section 1. Title and extent of operation of the Code: Gives the name of the law and where it applies.
Example: The IPC applies in all states and territories of India.

Section 2. Punishment of offences committed within India: IPC applies to all crimes committed within India, regardless of nationality.
Example: Theft committed in Delhi by an Indian citizen.
Punishment: As per the specific offence

Section 3. Punishment of offences committed outside India: Certain offences outside India are punishable if committed by an Indian citizen or affect Indian interests.
Example: Fraud against an Indian citizen from abroad.
Punishment: Same as if committed in India

Section 4. Extension of Code to extra-territorial offences: IPC applies to acts on Indian ships or aircraft even outside India.
Example: Crime on an Indian ship in international waters.

Section 5. Certain laws not to be affected by this Code: IPC does not override other special laws like military law or customs law.
Example: Offence committed by a soldier may be tried under military law.
Punishment: As per the special law

Conclusion
Sections 1 to 5 of IPC define the territorial and personal applicability of criminal law in India.
They ensure that offenders are punished even for crimes committed outside India in certain cases.

2.3 General Exceptions
IPC Chapter IV: Sections 76 - 106

Introduction
Chapter IV of the Indian Penal Code deals with General Exceptions.
These sections describe situations where an act, though appearing to be an offence, is not punishable.
They are based on the principle “Actus non facit reum nisi mens sit rea”.

Section 76. Act done by a person bound, or by mistake of fact: An act is **not an offence** if done by a person who is **bound by law** or acts under a **mistake of fact** without intent to commit a crime.
Example: Breaking a door to save a child from fire.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 77. Act of a child under seven years of age: Children **below 7 years** cannot be held criminally responsible.
Example: A 6-year-old accidentally hits someone.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 78. Act of a child above seven and under twelve of immature understanding: Children aged **7–12** are not criminally liable if they **lack sufficient understanding**.
Example: An 8-year-old unknowingly causes minor injury.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 79. Act done by a person of unsound mind: Acts by a person who is **insane** at the time of committing the act are not offences.
Example: Mentally unsound person injures someone during a delusion.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 80. Accident in doing a lawful act: Acts done **by accident** while performing a lawful act are not offences.
Example: Hitting someone by accident while cutting wood.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 81. Act likely to cause harm, but done without criminal intent, and to prevent other harm: Acts done to **avoid greater harm** are excused.
Example: Breaking someone’s window to escape a fire.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 82. Act of a person incapable of judgment due to intoxication: A person is **not criminally liable** if they are incapable of understanding the nature of their act due to **no voluntary intoxication**.
Example: Involuntary intoxication causes accidental harm.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 83. Act not intended and done by mistake: Acts done **without intention** and by mistake are not offences.
Example: Picking someone else’s bag by mistake.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 84. Act of a person of unsound mind: Repeats Section 79 – acts done by **mentally unsound persons** are not offences.
Example: Harming someone during a psychotic episode.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 85. Act done under compulsion: Acts done under **lawful compulsion or threat** are not offences.
Example: Threatened to commit theft under coercion.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 86. Offence requiring a particular intent: No offence if **intent is missing**.
Example: Accidentally causing damage without intention.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 87. Act not intended to cause death, done by consent in good faith for benefit: Harm done **with consent for benefit** (like medical treatment) is excused.
Example: Surgery performed with patient consent.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 88. Act not intended to cause death, done by consent for benefit: Similar to Section 87 – acts done **with consent for benefit** are not offences.
Example: Tattooing with consent.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 89. Act done to prevent offence: Acts done **to prevent other offences** are excused.
Example: Damaging property to stop a riot.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 90. Consent given by a person under fear or misconception: Consent obtained by **fear or misrepresentation** is invalid.
Example: Signing contract under threat.
Punishment: Offence as per law

Section 91. Exclusion of acts done in good faith for benefit of others: Acts done **in good faith for someone’s benefit** are excused.
Example: Administering medicine to a child without malice.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 92. Act done in good faith, for benefit of person without consent: Excuses acts done **without consent** if intended for benefit.
Example: Doctor treating unconscious patient.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 93. Communication of intent to cause harm: Threats made **without intent to carry them out** are excused.
Example: Warning someone to stop a harmful act.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 94. Acts causing slight harm, done in good faith: Minor harm done **in good faith for benefit or necessity** is excused.
Example: Pinching to prevent a child from running into danger.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 95. Acts done to prevent danger to person or property: Acts done **to prevent danger** are excused.
Example: Breaking a gate to save animals from fire.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 96. Things done in private defence: Acts done in **self-defence** are not offences.
Example: Hitting an attacker to protect oneself.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 97. Right of private defence of the body and property: Every person has right to defend **body and property** against harm.
Example: Protecting house from burglar.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 98. Acts against person doing lawful authority: Acts done **against a lawful authority** under certain restrictions.
Example: Resisting arrest without justification may be punished.
Punishment: As per IPC

Section 99. Acts against person doing lawful authority, not wrongful: Acts done in **private defence** against excess use of authority are excused.
Example: Resisting unlawful force used by officer.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 100. When the right of private defence of the body extends to causing death: Right to self-defence may **extend to causing death** if necessary to prevent grievous harm.
Example: Killing attacker who threatens life.
Punishment: Not applicable if proportionate

Section 101. Commencement and continuation of the right of private defence: Private defence begins **as soon as threat arises** and lasts while danger exists.
Example: Fending off an ongoing assault.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 102. Commencement of the right of private defence against property: Right to defend **property starts immediately** on threat.
Example: Preventing theft of goods.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 103. Continuation of the right of private defence against property: Defence lasts **while threat persists**.
Example: Chasing thief until property is safe.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 104. Right of private defence against deadly assault: Defence against **grievous threats** can be proportionate.
Example: Using force against armed attacker.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 105. Right of private defence against arrest or wrongful confinement: Can resist **unlawful arrest or confinement**.
Example: Escaping illegal detention.
Punishment: Not applicable

Section 106. Right of private defence against certain acts of deadly assault: Defence is allowed to prevent **serious harm or death**.
Example: Fighting back to prevent murder.
Punishment: Not applicable

Conclusion
General Exceptions protect persons acting in good faith, necessity, or lawful authority.
They ensure that innocent acts are not punished under criminal law.

2.4 Abetment
IPC Chapter V: Sections 107 120

Introduction
Abetment means encourage , engaging, or aiding a person to commit an offence.
A person who abets an offence is punishable even if he does not commit the main offence himself.

Section 107. Abetment of a thing: A person **abets** when they **instigate, engage, or intentionally help** another to commit an offence.
Example: Encouraging someone to commit theft or providing tools to do so.
Punishment: Same as if the person committed the offence, as per IPC

Section 108. Abetment of a thing by doing an act: Abetment occurs if someone **does an act** that intentionally aids the offence.
Example: Holding a door open for a burglar.
Punishment: Same as main offence

Section 109. Punishment of abetment if the act is committed in consequence and where no express provision is made for its punishment: If the abetted offence **occurs**, and IPC doesn’t specify punishment for abetment, the abettor is **punished like the offender**.
Example: Encouraging assault; assault occurs.
Punishment: As per the main offence

Section 110. Abetment of offence by **one person, not intended to commit**: If the abettor intends offence but the other **does not commit it**, abetment may still be punishable.
Example: Persuading someone to commit theft, but they back out.
Punishment: May be liable to lesser punishment

Section 111. Abetment of offence by **one person, by the act of another person**: Abetment includes cases where the **abetter causes another to commit an act**.
Example: Ordering someone to harm another; they do it.
Punishment: Same as main offence

Section 112. Abetment of offence by **concealment**: Hiding facts to aid offence is also abetment.
Example: Hiding evidence for a burglar.
Punishment: As per IPC

Section 113. Abetment of offence by **criminal conspiracy**: When a **criminal conspiracy exists**, everyone is liable if the act is committed.
Example: Planning robbery with others; robbery occurs.
Punishment: Same as main offence

Section 114. Abetment when an act is **not committed**: Abetment may still be punishable even if the offence **does not occur**, if intent is clear.
Example: Persuading theft that is never carried out.
Punishment: Lesser punishment as per law

Section 115. Abetment of an offence against the **sovereignty of India**: Abetment of crimes against the state is a serious offence.
Example: Encouraging rebellion or sedition.
Punishment: As per IPC (may include life imprisonment)

Section 116. Abetment of **insult with intent to provoke breach of peace**: Abetment of acts causing public disorder is punishable.
Example: Encouraging communal riots.
Punishment: Imprisonment or fine as per IPC

Section 117. Abetment of **suicide of a minor or insane person**: Abetment of vulnerable person’s suicide is punishable.
Example: Forcing a minor to commit suicide.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years

Section 118. Abetment of **offence by public servant**: Abetment of crimes by public servants in office is specially punishable.
Example: Encouraging bribery by a government official.
Punishment: As per IPC

Section 119. Abetment of an offence **not committed**: Abetment punishable even if offence not committed, under certain conditions.
Example: Persuading theft that is not carried out.
Punishment: Lesser punishment

Section 120. Definition of criminal conspiracy: When **two or more persons agree to commit an illegal act**, it is a criminal conspiracy.
Example: Planning a robbery together.
Punishment: As per IPC; may be same as abetted offence

Conclusion
Abetment ensures that all persons involved in a crime are punished.
It covers those who instigate, plan, or assist in committing offences.

2.5 Criminal Conspiracy
IPC Chapter V-A: Sections 120 A & B

Section 120A. Definition of criminal conspiracy: When **two or more persons agree** to commit an illegal act, they are said to have formed a **criminal conspiracy**.
Example: A group plans to rob a bank together.
Punishment: Not punishable by itself; acts committed in furtherance are punishable under 120B

Section 120B. Punishment of criminal conspiracy: If a criminal conspiracy **results in the commission of an offence**, all conspirators are **liable for punishment as if they committed the offence themselves**.
Example: If the planned bank robbery is executed, all conspirators can be punished, even if some did not directly participate.
Punishment: Same as punishment for the offence committed in consequence of the conspiracy; can include imprisonment, fine, or both

Conclusion – Criminal Conspiracy (Sections 120A & 120B)

- A criminal conspiracy arises when two or more persons agree to commit an illegal act or a legal act by illegal means.
- Agreement alone is sufficient; actual commission of the crime is not necessary.
- Punishment under Section 120B depends on the intended crime:
  - Minor crimes → lesser punishment
  - Serious crimes (death/life) → punishment as prescribed for the offense.
- Key Principle: “Agreement with criminal intent makes all parties liable, even if the act is not completed.”

2.6 Attempts
IPC Chapter XXIII: Section 511

1. Definition of Attempt (Section 511)
- Who it applies to: Anyone who attempts to commit a crime but does not succeed.
- What it says: "Whoever attempts to commit an offense punishable by law but does not succeed, shall be punished as if they had committed the offense, to the extent of the punishment prescribed for attempting it."
- Key: Intention + overt act to commit the crime is enough; success is not required.

2. Components of Attempt
1. Intention: The person must have the intent to commit a crime.
2. Overt Act: There must be a direct act towards committing the offense. Mere preparation is not enough.
3. Failure or Non-completion: The crime is not successfully completed.

Punishment under Section 511 – IPC

1. General Rule: Lesser Punishment for Attempt
- If someone tries to commit a crime but fails, the law usually gives a lighter punishment than if they had succeeded.
- Often, the punishment is half of the maximum punishment for that offense.
- Example:
  Theft (Sec 378) → Maximum punishment 3 years
  Attempted theft → Up to 1.5 years (half of 3 years)

2. Exception: Serious Crimes (Life or Death)
- If the crime attempted is very serious (punishable by life imprisonment or death), the law does not automatically halve the punishment.
- Instead, the attempt is punishable as prescribed in the IPC for attempting that crime.
- Example:
  Attempted murder (Sec 307) → Even if victim survives, punishment can be severe (up to the full extent for attempt)
  Attempted murder leading to death → Full murder punishment if death occurs

Section 511. Punishment for attempting to commit an offence: A person who **attempts to commit a crime**, but does not succeed, can be punished **as if they committed the offence**, but generally with **lesser severity**.
Example: Trying to break into a house for theft but getting caught before stealing.
Punishment: Imprisonment or fine, **up to half of the maximum punishment** for the actual offence

Joint criminal liability in Unlawful Assembly (Sections 141–147): All members of an **unlawful assembly** are liable for acts committed in **furtherance of the common objective**, even if they did not individually commit the act.
Example: A mob armed with sticks damages property; each member can be punished.
Punishment: As per the offence committed, proportionate to participation

Joint criminal liability in Gang Rape (Sections 376D & 34): When **multiple persons commit rape together**, each is **equally liable** under Section 34 (common intention).
Example: Three persons assault and rape a victim together; all are punished as principals.
Punishment: Life imprisonment, fine, or death penalty in extreme cases

Joint criminal liability in Dacoity (Sections 391–395 & 34): If **five or more persons commit robbery together**, all are liable as **principal offenders**.
Example: A gang of six robs a bank using weapons; even the person who only helps in planning is liable.
Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment for 10 years to life, or fine, as per IPC

3. Easy Way to Remember
- Minor crimes only attempt(not succeed): Punishment = Usually half of the original crime
- Major crimes (life/death)only attempt(not succeed): Punishment = As prescribed; no automatic reduction 4. Important Points
- Mere preparation is not punishable; there must be a clear step towards committing the crime.
- Intention + act = attempt
- Applies to all offenses under IPC, unless the law specifically excludes attempts.

5. Examples
- A points a gun to shoot B but B escapes → Attempt to murder (sec. 307)
- A tries to steal a bike but is caught → Attempt to theft (sec. 511)

Overt Act, Success Not Required

Conclusion – Attempts (Section 511)

- An attempt occurs when a person has intention to commit a crime and does an overt act toward it but fails to complete it.
- Success is not required; intention + overt act = punishable attempt.
- Punishment under Section 511:
  - Minor crimes → usually half of the maximum punishment.
  - Serious crimes (life/death) → punishment as prescribed for the attempted offense.
- Key Principle: “Even an unsuccessful attempt is punishable if the offender intended and acted to commit the crime.”

1. Unlawful Assembly, Gang Rape and Dacoity from point of view of Joint criminal liability

1. Basics
- Joint criminal liability: When 2 or more persons commit a crime together, all are liable.
- Based on common intention (Sec 34) or common object (Sec 149).

2. Unlawful Assembly (Sec 141)
- Definition: 5 or more persons with common object to commit an offense.
- Liability: All members liable for acts done in furtherance of common object.
- Example: 5 people riot, one destroys property → all 5 liable.

3. Gang Rape (Sec 376D)
- Definition: Sexual assault by 2 or more persons.
- Liability: All participants liable for the entire act under common intention (Sec 34).
- Example: 3 men assault a woman → all charged with gang rape.

4. Dacoity (Sec 391)
- Definition: Robbery by 5 or more persons.
- Liability: All members liable for acts done in course of dacoity (common object under Sec 149).
- Example: 6 persons commit dacoity, one kills guard → all 6 liable for murder.

Conclusion – Joint Criminal Liability

- When multiple persons act together to commit a crime, each can be held equally responsible for the acts done in furtherance of the common intention or common object.
- In Unlawful Assembly and Dacoity, liability arises from common object (Sec 149).
- In Gang Rape, liability arises from common intention (Sec 34).
- Even a minor participant cannot escape punishment if the act committed was in furtherance of the joint plan.
- Key Principle: “Acting together with a common purpose makes all members responsible, regardless of individual role.”

Example: Using counterfeit revenue stamps on legal documents.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 235. Using forged government stamps: Passing or applying **fake stamps** knowingly.
Example: Affixing forged stamp on property sale deed.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 236. Possession of forged government stamps: Keeping forged stamps for fraudulent purposes.
Example: Stocking fake tax stamps for illegal use.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 years, and fine

Section 237. Forgery of public seals or marks: Forging official seals, marks, or symbols.
Example: Creating fake official seal of government office.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 238. Using forged public seals or marks: Applying or using **fake seals** knowingly.
Example: Sealing documents with forged government seal.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 239–263A. Various offences including **counterfeiting, using, possessing, buying, selling, or importing fake currency, stamps, and government marks**, and offences relating to **coins, banknotes, and government stamps**.
Example: Producing or distributing fake currency notes or government bonds.
Punishment: Varies as per section; can include imprisonment for life, rigorous imprisonment for several years, and fine

3.8 Offences relating to weights and measures
IPC Chapter XIII: Sections 264 – 267

Section 264. Sale of goods at less than the correct weight or measure: Selling goods with **incorrect weight or measure**, intending to cheat.

Section 265. Using false weights or measures: Employing **fraudulent weights or measures** to deceive buyers.

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 6 months, or fine, or both

Section 266. Making or selling false weights or measures: Producing or selling **fake weights or measures** for fraudulent use.
Example: Selling tampered measuring jars to shopkeepers.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 6 months, or fine, or both

Section 267. Fraudulent use of weights or measures: Knowingly **using fraudulent weights or measures** in trade.
Example: A shopkeeper continues using a rigged balance to cheat customers.

Conclusion: Sections 264–267 of the IPC protect **fair trade and public interest** by penalizing fraud in weights and measures. These laws ensure **honesty in commercial transactions** and prevent exploitation of consumers. 3.9 Offences affecting the human body
IPC Chapter XVI: Sections 299 – 377

Section 299. Culpable homicide: Causing death **with knowledge or intention** that it may cause death.
Example: Striking someone with intent to kill and causing death.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to life, or death penalty

Section 300. Murder: Culpable homicide with **specific intent or circumstances** defined by law.
Example: Premeditated killing of a person.
Punishment: Death or life imprisonment

Section 304. Punishment for culpable homicide not amounting to murder: Killing **without the intent to cause death**.
Example: Hitting someone in anger leading to accidental death.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years, or fine, or both

Section 307. Attempt to murder: **Attempting to cause death** with intention.
Example: Stabbing someone with intent to kill but they survive.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years, and fine

Section 312. Causing miscarriage: Intentionally causing **termination of pregnancy** without consent.
Example: Administering drugs to cause abortion without consent.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 years, or fine, or both

Section 316. Death of quick unborn child by act amounting to culpable homicide of mother: Causing death of **viable unborn child**.
Example: Injuring pregnant woman leading to child’s death.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years

Section 319. Hurt: Causing **bodily pain, disease, or infirmity**.
Example: Slapping or injuring someone causing temporary pain.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine, or both

Section 320. Grievous hurt: Severe bodily injury such as **loss of limb, sight, hearing, or deformity**.
Example: Cutting off a finger intentionally.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 321. Voluntarily causing hurt: **Intentionally causing hurt** without aggravating circumstances.
Example: Punching someone during a fight.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine, or both

Section 325. Punishment for voluntarily causing grievous hurt: Causing **serious injury intentionally**.
Example: Stabbing someone, causing permanent injury.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years, and fine

Section 326. Voluntarily causing grievous hurt by dangerous weapons or means: Injuring someone with **weapons or dangerous objects**.
Example: Hitting someone with a knife causing permanent injury.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years, and fine

Section 354. Assault or criminal force to woman with intent to outrage her modesty: Sexual assault or **outraging the modesty** of a woman.
Example: Molesting a woman in public.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 years, or fine, or both

Section 375. Rape: Sexual intercourse **without consent**.
Example: Forcibly having sexual relations with a woman against her will.
Punishment: Imprisonment not less than 7 years, may extend to life, and fine

Section 376. Punishment for rape: Penalties for **rape**.
Example: Conviction for non-consensual sexual act.
Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment not less than 7 years, may extend to life, and fine

Section 377. Unnatural offences: **Sexual acts against nature** without consent.
Example: Non-consensual sexual acts with human or animal.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years, and fine

Conclusion: Sections 299–377 of IPC aim to **protect human life, bodily integrity, and personal dignity**. They cover offences from **culpable homicide and murder** to **grievous hurt, sexual offences, and unnatural acts**, ensuring **justice, deterrence, and protection for victims**. The severity of punishment varies based on **intention, degree of harm, and use of weapons**, highlighting the importance of **intent and harm in criminal liability**.

IPC Chapter I: Sections 171A – 171I

Example: Conviction for bribery, personation, or undue influence.

4.3 Offences relating to religion
IPC Chapter XV: Sections 295 – 298

Section 295. Injuring or defiling place of worship with intent to insult religion: **Deliberately damaging or defiling a place of worship** to insult the religion of any class.
Example: Damaging a temple or mosque intentionally to insult followers.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 2 years, or fine, or both

Section 295A. Deliberate and malicious acts intended to outrage religious feelings: **Deliberately insulting religion** through words, writing, or visible representation.
Example: Publishing materials intentionally offending religious sentiments.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 years, or fine, or both

Section 296. Disturbing religious assembly: **Voluntarily disturbing a religious gathering** lawfully assembled.
Example: Interrupting a congregation during prayers.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine, or both

Section 297. Trespassing on burial places, etc., with intent to insult religion: **Entering cemeteries, cremation grounds, or sacred places** to insult religion or offend feelings.
Example: Entering a graveyard to desecrate it intentionally.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine, or both

Section 298. Uttering words, etc., with deliberate intent to wound religious feelings: **Speaking, writing, or using gestures** to outrage religious feelings.
Example: Making offensive statements about religious beliefs in public.
Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine, or both

Conclusion: Sections 295–298 of IPC aim to **protect religious sentiments and maintain communal harmony**. They penalize acts that **insult religion, disturb worship, or wound religious feelings**, emphasizing **respect, tolerance, and peaceful coexistence** in a diverse society.
