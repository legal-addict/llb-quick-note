Philosophy of Constitution

1. What is a Constitution?
- It is the rulebook of the country.
- Tells how the government works.
- Protects the rights of people.

2. Why do we have a Constitution?
- To give power to the government legally.
- To make sure government does not become unfair.
- To protect citizens’ rights.
- To keep everyone equal and safe.

3. Main Goals (Philosophy) of Constitution
1. Make Government work properly.
2. Limit Government power so it cannot misuse it.
3. Protect Fundamental Rights (freedom, equality, justice).
4. Promote Justice (social, economic, political).
5. Ensure Rule of Law – everyone must follow the law.
6. Ensure Democracy – people have a say in government.

4. Features of Constitution
- Supreme Law – highest law of the country.
- Can be written or based on customs.
- May be easy or hard to change.
- Separates powers: Legislature, Executive, Judiciary.
- Gives Fundamental Rights to citizens.
- Gives Directive Principles – guides government to help people.

5. Types of Constitution Philosophy
- Liberal – focus on individual freedom (example: USA).
- Social Welfare – focus on helping poor and equality (example: India).
- Democratic – people have the power through elections.

6. Why Philosophy is Important?
- Helps interpret laws correctly.
- Keeps balance between rights and duties.
- Ensures fairness and justice.

7. Easy Memory Tip
G-P-R-J = Government, Protection, Rights, Justice
- G – Government power
- P – Protect people
- R – Rights of citizens
- J – Justice for all
Constitution as a Basic Norm

1. Meaning:
- The Constitution is the **fundamental rule** of the country.
- All laws and government actions must follow it.
- It is like the **foundation of a building**; everything else depends on it.

2. Basic Norm Concept (Kelsen)
- A famous jurist, **Hans Kelsen**, said: The Constitution is the **"basic norm" (Grundnorm)**.
- It means: All laws are valid **because the Constitution exists**.
- Even if we pass a new law, it is valid **only if it follows the Constitution**.

3. Features of Constitution as Basic Norm
1. It is **supreme** – no law can go against it.
2. It gives **authority** to make other laws.
3. It provides **rules for government powers**.
4. It protects **citizens’ fundamental rights**.
5. It ensures **justice, liberty, and equality** in the country.

4. Importance:
- Keeps government **within limits**.
- Ensures **rule of law**.
- Acts as a **reference for courts** to check if laws are valid.
- Helps maintain **stability and fairness** in society.

5. Easy Way to Remember:
- Constitution = Basic Norm = **Foundation of all laws**.
- If a law goes against the Constitution → it is **invalid**.
Rule of Law and Constitutionalism

1. Rule of Law – Meaning:
- Rule of Law means **no one is above the law**, not even the government.
- Everyone must **follow the law**.
- Law is **fair, known, and applied equally** to all.

2. Key Points of Rule of Law:
1. Law applies to **everyone equally**.
2. Government must act **according to law**.
3. **Justice and fairness** must be ensured.
4. Prevents **arbitrary or unfair use of power**.
5. Ensures **individual rights are protected**.

3. Constitutionalism – Meaning:
- Constitutionalism means the **government must follow the Constitution**.
- Government powers are **limited by law**.
- Constitution is **supreme**, and government cannot violate it.

4. Key Points of Constitutionalism:
1. Constitution limits government power.
2. Protects **fundamental rights** of citizens.
3. Ensures **separation of powers** (Legislature, Executive, Judiciary).
4. Promotes **democracy and justice**.

5. Difference Between Rule of Law and Constitutionalism:
- Rule of Law = Everyone, including government, must follow the law.
- Constitutionalism = Government is **bound by the Constitution** specifically.
- Both ensure **justice, fairness, and protection of rights**.

6. Importance:
- Prevents misuse of power.
- Maintains **order and fairness** in society.
- Protects **citizens’ rights**.
- Ensures **government accountability**.

7. Easy Way to Remember:
- Rule of Law = Law applies to all.
- Constitutionalism = Government must follow Constitution.
Constitution as a Living Document

1. Meaning:
- A living constitution means it can **adapt to changing times**.
- It is **not fixed**; it can grow with society.
- The constitution **remains relevant** even as society, technology, and values change.

2. Why it is called “Living”?
1. Society and technology are always changing.
2. Laws made long ago may need **new interpretation** today.
3. Courts interpret the Constitution in a way that **fits current needs**.
4. Ensures the Constitution is **practical, not just theoretical**.

3. Key Features:
- Flexible and adaptable.
- Allows judicial interpretation according to **modern issues**.
- Supports social justice, equality, and liberty in **changing contexts**.

4. Importance:
- Keeps the Constitution **relevant over time**.
- Protects citizens’ rights in **new situations**.
- Ensures democracy and justice **work in modern society**.

5. Example:
- Right to privacy or digital rights are recognized today even if not mentioned in original text.

6. Easy Way to Remember:
- Constitution = Living Document → **Adapts and changes with society**.
Forms and Models of Constitution

1. Forms of Constitution
- Forms explain **how government is organized and power is distributed**.

A. Written vs Unwritten Constitution
1. Written Constitution:
- All rules are **written in one document**.
- Example: India, USA.
2. Unwritten Constitution:
- Rules come from **customs, conventions, and past decisions**.
- Example: UK.

B. Flexible vs Rigid Constitution
1. Flexible:
- Can be changed like **ordinary laws**.
- Example: UK.
2. Rigid:
- **Difficult to change**; special procedure needed.
- Example: USA, India.

C. Federal vs Unitary Constitution
1. Federal:
- Power is shared between **central and state governments**.
- Example: India, USA.
2. Unitary:
- Power is **concentrated in central government**.
- Example: UK, France.

2. Models of Constitution
- Models explain **the way power is exercised and organized**.

1. Parliamentary Model
- Executive (Prime Minister + Ministers) comes from Parliament.
- Government depends on Parliament to stay in power.
- Head of State (President or Monarch) is mostly ceremonial.
- Example: India, UK.
- Easy way to remember: Parliament chooses government; government can fall if it loses support.

2. Presidential Model
- President is elected separately and is independent of legislature.
- President is both Head of State and Head of Government.
- Legislature cannot remove President easily.
- Example: USA.
- Easy way to remember: President is boss; Parliament cannot fire them.

3. Hybrid / Semi-Presidential Model
- Combines Parliamentary and Presidential features.
- Has both President and Prime Minister.
- President has special powers; PM runs day-to-day government.
- Example: France, Russia.
- Easy way to remember: Two bosses — President for big decisions, PM for daily work.
3. Easy Way to Remember:
- Forms = **how constitution is written and power is divided**.
- Models = **how government works and powers are exercised**.

Historical Background and Framing of the Indian Constitution

1. Need for a Constitution in India
- India needed a **written law** after independence in 1947.
- To **replace British laws** and ensure **self-rule and democracy**.
- To **protect citizens’ rights** and create a **sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic**.

2. Historical Background
- 1858: British Crown took control after 1857 Revolt.
- 1909: Morley-Minto Reforms introduced limited Indian participation in governance.
- 1919: Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms expanded legislative councils.
- 1935: Government of India Act – proposed **federal structure**.
- 1942: Quit India Movement – demand for independence intensified.
- 1946: Cabinet Mission Plan – proposed framework for a **united India**.

3. Constituent Assembly
- Formed in **1946** to draft the Constitution.
- Members elected indirectly by **provincial legislatures**.
- Headed by **Dr. Rajendra Prasad (President)**.
- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** was the Chairman of the Drafting Committee.

4. Framing of the Constitution
- Drafting Committee prepared the **draft Constitution**.
- Discussions held in **English** in Assembly sessions.
- Adopted on **26th November 1949**.
- Came into effect on **26th January 1950** (Republic Day).

5. Features Influenced by History
- Parliamentary system from **UK**.
- Fundamental Rights inspired by **USA**.
- Directive Principles inspired by **Ireland**.
- Judicial review inspired by **USA and Canada**.

Legal System during East India Company’s Rule in India

1. Introduction
- The East India Company (1600–1858) was **a trading company** that gradually became the ruler of India.
- It **introduced its own laws** to govern British territories in India.
- Indian laws were influenced by **British law, local customs, and religious laws**.

2. Early Legal System
- Initially, the Company **followed local customary laws** for Indians.
- For British subjects, **English law** was applied.
- There was **no uniform legal system** across India.

3. Courts Established
1. **Mayor’s Court** (Madras 1726, Bombay 1726, Calcutta 1727) – dealt with civil and criminal cases involving Europeans.
2. **Supreme Court of Judicature at Fort William** (Calcutta 1774) – higher court for British subjects.
3. **Diwani Courts** – Company collected revenue; local laws applied in civil matters.

4. Legal Sources
- **Customary law** – local traditions and practices.
- **Religious law** – Hindu and Muslim personal laws.
- **Regulations by Company** – laws made by Company officials and governors.
- **English law** – applied to Europeans and gradually influenced Indian laws.

5. Key Features
- Dual system: British law for Europeans, customary/religious law for Indians.
- Focus on **revenue collection and maintaining order**.
- Gradual **introduction of codified laws** in the 19th century (e.g., Indian Penal Code).

6. Importance
- Laid the **foundation of modern Indian legal system**.
- Introduced **courts, judges, codified laws**, and legal procedures.
- Influenced **later reforms by the British government after 1858**.

Legal System during British Crown’s Rule in India (1858–1947)

1. Introduction
- After 1857 Revolt, **British Crown took over from East India Company**.
- Queen Victoria’s Proclamation (1858) ensured **direct rule** of India by the Crown.
- Legal system became **more organized and uniform** compared to Company rule.

2. Administration of Justice
- Created **High Courts** in major provinces (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, 1862).
- Introduced **lower courts** at district and sub-district levels.
- Established **civil, criminal, and revenue courts**.
- Judges trained in **British law**.

3. Legal Sources
- **Codified laws**: Indian Penal Code (1860), Criminal Procedure Code, Civil Procedure Code.
- **British common law** applied to European residents.
- **Customary and personal laws** applied to Indians in family and inheritance matters.
- Acts passed by **Legislative Councils** of provinces and later Central Legislature.

4. Key Features
- **Uniform legal system** across British India.
- Focus on **codification** – clear, written laws.
- Introduced **appeal system**: District Court → High Court → Privy Council (London).
- Separation of **judiciary from executive** (partly).

5. Importance
- Laid **modern legal foundations** for India.
- Created **structured courts and professional judiciary**.
- Introduced **rule of law and codified laws** for easier governance.
- Influenced post-independence legal system in India.

Freedom Struggle- Simon’s Commission, Communal Award, Civil disobedience movement. Freedom Struggle: Key Events

1. Simon Commission (1927)
- British government set up a commission to review the working of **Government of India Act, 1919**.
- **No Indian members** were included → widely opposed.
- Indians protested with the slogan: "**Simon Go Back**".
- Result: Rise of **nationalist unity** and demand for **complete self-rule**.

2. Communal Award (1932)
- Announced by **British Prime Minister Ramsay MacDonald**.
- Provided **separate electorates** for minorities: Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, and Dalits (Scheduled Castes).
- Controversial because it **divided Indian society** on religio us and caste lines.
- Opposed by Mahatma Gandhi → **Poona Pact**: reserved seats for Dalits within **Hindu electorate**.

3. Civil Disobedience Movement (1930–1934)
- Led by **Mahatma Gandhi** to protest **British salt tax** and colonial laws.
- Famous events:
- **Salt March (Dandi March)** – 1930.
- Boycott of British goods, schools, courts, and taxes.
- Aim: **non-violent protest for full independence**.
- Result: Arrest of leaders, but increased **nationalist sentiment and participation**.

4. Easy Way to Remember
- Simon Commission → No Indians → Protests
- Communal Award → Minorities’ separate electorates → Poona Pact
- Civil Disobedience → Salt March → Non-violent resistance
Mountbatten’s Plan 1947 and Indian Independence Act 1947. Mountbatten’s Plan 1947 & Indian Independence Act 1947

1. Mountbatten’s Plan (June 3, 1947)
- Proposed by **Lord Mountbatten**, the last British Viceroy of India.
- Aim: **Smooth transfer of power from British to Indians**.
- Key points:
1. **Partition of British India** into India and Pakistan.
2. **Provinces could choose** to join India or Pakistan (referendum in Punjab and Bengal).
3. **Princely States** given freedom to join either country.
4. **Transition of power** on August 15, 1947.
5. **Communal harmony** and protection of minorities emphasized.

2. Indian Independence Act 1947
- Passed by **British Parliament** on July 18, 1947.
- **Legal basis** for India and Pakistan to become independent on **August 15, 1947**.
- Key points:
1. **Ended British rule** in India.
2. **Created two dominions**: India and Pakistan.
3. **Governor-General** as representative of British Crown for initial years.
4. **Princely States** free to join either dominion.
5. **British Parliament no longer had authority** in India.

3. Difference between Mountbatten Plan and Independence Act
- Mountbatten Plan = **proposed plan by Viceroy**.
- Independence Act = **law passed by British Parliament** to implement the plan.

4. Easy Way to Remember:
- Mountbatten Plan → Plan for partition and transfer of power.
- Indian Independence Act → Legal law to make India & Pakistan independent on Aug 15, 1947.

1.3- Preamble

What is the Preamble?
It tells us why the Constitution was made, what it aims to achieve, and the values India stands for.

Preamble: Socio-Legal Concepts



Definition: Socio-legal concepts are the principles in the Constitution that connect law with society. They show how law should achieve social justice, equality, and welfare.

Key Socio-Legal Concepts in the Preamble:
Sovereign – India is independent in decision-making; no external control.
Socialist – Promotes social and economic equality; reduces inequalities.
Secular – No state religion; all religions are equal before the law.
Democratic – People are the source of power; elected representatives govern.
Republic – Head of state (President) is elected, not hereditary.
Justice – Includes social, economic, and political justice.
Liberty – Freedom of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship.
Equality – Equal rights, opportunities, and status for all.
Fraternity – Promotes national unity and dignity of the individual.

Preamble as a Tool to Interpret the Constitution

Judicial Pronouncements on the Preamble

Meaning and Importance of the Preamble
The Preamble reflects the objectives, philosophy, and ideals of the Indian Constitution.
It acts as a guiding tool for interpreting constitutional provisions.

Key Judicial Pronouncements

1. Berubari Union Case (1960)
Issue: Whether the Preamble is part of the Constitution.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that the Preamble is not a part of the Constitution.
It is not a source of power but can help in interpretation.

2. Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
Issue: Whether Parliament can amend the Preamble.
Judgment: The Court held that the Preamble is a part of the Constitution.
Parliament can amend it under Article 368, but it cannot destroy the Basic Structure.
This case introduced the Basic Structure Doctrine.

3. Indira Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)
Judgment: Democracy, free and fair elections, and rule of law are part of the basic structure.
Any law violating these principles is unconstitutional.

4. Minerva Mills Case (1980)
Judgment: The Preamble is the key to understanding the Constitution.
Balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles is part of the basic structure.

5. S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)
Judgment: Secularism mentioned in the Preamble is a basic feature of the Constitution.
State actions violating secularism can be struck down.

Present Legal Position
The Preamble is part of the Constitution.
It can be amended.
It cannot be amended to destroy the basic structure.
It serves as a guiding light for judicial interpretation.

Conclusion
The Preamble is an integral part of the Indian Constitution and plays a vital role in constitutional interpretation, subject to the Basic Structure Doctrine.

1.4- Salient Features of the Indian Constitution.

A salient feature means makes something special or different.

1. Lengthiest Written Constitution
The Indian Constitution is the longest written constitution in the world.
It contains detailed provisions about the structure, powers, and functions of the government.

2. Drawn from Various Sources
The Constitution has borrowed features from many countries like:
Britain (Parliamentary system),
USA (Fundamental Rights),
Ireland (Directive Principles),
Canada (Federal system).

3. Federal System with Unitary Bias
India has a federal system with division of powers between the Centre and States.
However, during emergencies, it becomes unitary in nature with more power to the Centre.

4. Parliamentary Form of Government
India follows the Parliamentary system.
The President is the nominal head, while the Prime Minister is the real executive head.
The executive is responsible to the legislature.

5. Fundamental Rights
The Constitution guarantees Fundamental Rights to all citizens (Articles 12–35).
These rights ensure equality, freedom, and justice.
Courts protect these rights through judicial review.

6. Directive Principles of State Policy
Directive Principles guide the State in making laws.
They aim to establish a welfare state.
They are not enforceable by courts but are fundamental in governance.

7. Secular State
India is a secular country.
The State does not have any official religion.
All religions are treated equally.

8. Single Citizenship
India provides single citizenship to all citizens.
This promotes unity and integrity of the nation.

9. Independent Judiciary
The Constitution provides for an independent judiciary.
Judges are free from executive interference.
The judiciary acts as the guardian of the Constitution.

10. Universal Adult Franchise
Every citizen above 18 years has the right to vote.
There is no discrimination based on caste, religion, gender, or wealth.

Conclusion
The Indian Constitution is a unique blend of rigidity and flexibility.
Its salient features reflect the ideals of democracy, justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity.

1.5- Provisions relating to Citizenship and Citizenship Act 1955 (Part II).

Meaning of Citizenship
Citizenship refers to the legal relationship between an individual and the State.
A citizen enjoys civil and political rights and owes duties to the State.

Citizenship under the Indian Constitution (Part II: Articles 5–11)

Article 5 – Citizenship at the Commencement of the Constitution
A person was considered a citizen of India on 26 January 1950 if he/she:
Was domiciled in India, and
Was born in India or
Had parents born in India or
Had lived in India for at least 5 years before commencement.

Article 6 – Citizenship of Migrants from Pakistan
Persons who migrated from Pakistan to India were granted citizenship if:
They or their parents/grandparents were born in India, and
They satisfied conditions of residence or registration.

Article 7 – Citizenship of Migrants to Pakistan
Persons who migrated to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 were not considered citizens of India.
Exception: Those who returned to India with a permit for resettlement.

Article 8 – Citizenship of Indians Living Abroad
Persons of Indian origin living outside India could claim citizenship if:
They or their parents/grandparents were born in India, and
They were registered with an Indian diplomatic mission.
example: An Indian-origin person living in the UK goes to the Indian High Commission in London, applies for indian citizenship, and gets officially recorded.
Article 9 states that Indian citizenship is lost when a person voluntarily accepts the citizenship of another country.

Article 9 – Voluntary Acquisition of Foreign Citizenship
Any person who voluntarily acquires foreign citizenship ceases to be an Indian citizen.

Article 10 – Continuance of Citizenship
Every person who is a citizen under Part II shall continue to be a citizen of India.

Article 11 – Power of Parliament
Parliament has the power to make laws regarding acquisition, termination, and regulation of citizenship.

Citizenship Act, 1955
The Citizenship Act, 1955 was enacted under Article 11.
It provides detailed rules related to citizenship after 1950.

Modes of Acquisition of Indian Citizenship
Citizenship can be acquired by:
Birth
Descent
Registration
Naturalization: legal process by which a non-citizen voluntarily acquires citizenship of a country after birth
Incorporation of Territory:absorbing new territory

Modes of Termination of Citizenship
Citizenship can be lost by:
Renunciation (voluntarily giving up citizenship)
Termination (acquiring foreign citizenship)
Deprivation (taken away by the government under certain conditions)

Important Feature
India follows the principle of single citizenship.
There is no dual citizenship under Indian law.
Dual citizenship means a person is legally a citizen of two countries at the same time.br

Conclusion
Part II of the Constitution and the Citizenship Act, 1955 together explain who is an Indian citizen,
how citizenship is acquired, and
how it can be lost.

2.1 – Fundamental Rights: Origin, Purpose and Significance



Origin of Fundamental Rights
Fundamental Rights in India are inspired mainly by the American Constitution.
They are included in Part III (Articles 12–35) of the Indian Constitution.
They came into force on 26 January 1950.

Purpose of Fundamental Rights
To protect citizens from arbitrary actions (something is based on random choice) of the State.
To ensure liberty, equality, and justice.
To promote the overall development of individuals.

Significance of Fundamental Rights
They are essential for a democratic system.
They limit the powers of the government.
They are justiciable, meaning they can be enforced in courts.
They make the Constitution a living document.

Article 12 – Definition of State


Article 12 defines the term “State” for the purpose of Fundamental Rights.

State includes:
The Government and Parliament of India
The Government and Legislature of States
Local authorities (Municipalities, Panchayats)
Other authorities within India or under the control of the Government of India.

Concept of State Instrumentalities and Agencies
State instrumentalities are bodies that function as an arm of the government.
They may be created by statute or owned/controlled by the government.

Examples:
LIC, ONGC, BHEL, Airport Authority of India.

Tests to identify State instrumentalities (Judicial Interpretation)
Financial control by the government.
Administrative control by the government.
Performance of public functions.
Deep and pervasive state control.

Article 13 – Judicial Review



Article 13 gives power to courts to review laws and strike down those violating Fundamental Rights.

Judicial Review
It is the power of courts to declare a law unconstitutional if it violates Fundamental Rights.
Both Supreme Court and High Courts can exercise this power.

Pre-Constitutional Laws
Laws made before 26 January 1950.
If inconsistent with Fundamental Rights, they become void to the extent of inconsistency.

Post-Constitutional Laws
Laws made after 26 January 1950.
If they violate Fundamental Rights, they are void from the beginning.

Doctrine of Ultra Vires
Ultra Vires means “beyond the powers”.
If a law goes beyond the powers given by the Constitution, it is invalid.
Example:
If Parliament makes a law that violates Fundamental Rights,
the Supreme Court can declare it ultra vires and strike it down.

Doctrine of Eclipse
Applicable to pre-constitutional laws.
A law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights is not dead but remains inactive.
It becomes active again if the inconsistency is removed.
Example:
A law made in 1948 restricts freedom of speech.
After 1950, it becomes eclipsed due to Article 19.
If Article 19 is later amended, the old law can become active again.

Doctrine of Severability
If only a part of a law is unconstitutional, only that part is removed.
The remaining valid part continues to operate.
Example:
A law has 10 sections.
Section 3 violates a Fundamental Right.
The court strikes down only Section 3 and keeps the remaining sections valid.

Doctrine of Waiver
Waiver means giving up a right.
In India, a person cannot waive Fundamental Rights.
Fundamental Rights are for public interest, not just individual benefit.
Example:
“I agree not to ask for equal pay, even if I do the same work as others.”

Conclusion
Fundamental Rights act as the backbone of democracy.
Articles 12 and 13 ensure that the State remains within constitutional limits and protects individual liberty.

2.2 – Article 14–18: Right to Equality



Introduction
Articles 14–18 in Part III of the Constitution guarantee the Right to Equality.
These rights are fundamental to a democratic society.

Article 14 – Equality before Law and Equal Protection of Laws
- Equality before law: No person is above the law.
- Equal protection of laws: Everyone must be treated equally in similar situations.
- Exceptions: Reasonable classification is allowed if it is not arbitrary.
example:
Government Jobs:Only citizens of India can apply for certain government jobs.
Reasonable ✅ – Protects national interests.

Article 15 – Prohibition of Discrimination
- The State cannot discriminate against anyone on grounds of:
Religion, Race, Caste, Sex, Place of Birth.
- The State can make special provisions for:
- Women and children
- Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), and other backward classes (OBC)

Article 16 – Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment
- No citizen can be discriminated against in government jobs on grounds of religion, caste, sex, etc.
- Special provisions can be made for SCs, STs, and OBCs.

Article 17 – Abolition of Untouchability
- Untouchability is abolished.
- Practice of untouchability is punishable by law.

Article 18 – Abolition of Titles
- The State cannot confer any titles except for military or academic distinctions.
- This prevents creating social hierarchies based on titles.
Example:
- Before independence, some people were called “Raja” or “Sir” by the British.
- After Article 18, such hereditary or honorary titles cannot be legally used in India.
Exceptions:
- Military honors (like Param Vir Chakra) are allowed.
- Academic titles (like Doctor of Science) are allowed.

Conclusion
Articles 14–18 ensure social, political, and legal equality.
They protect individuals from arbitrary discrimination and promote justice and fairness in India.

2.3 – Article 19: Six Fundamental Freedoms and Reasonable Restrictions



Introduction:
Article 19 of the Indian Constitution guarantees certain fundamental freedoms to all citizens.
These freedoms are subject to reasonable restrictions in the interest of the sovereignty, security, and public order of the country.

Six Fundamental Freedoms under Article 19:
1. Freedom of Speech and Expression (Clause 1(a))
- Right to express opinions freely through speech, writing, or any medium.
Restriction Example: Cannot spread fake news or hate speech that causes riots.

2. Freedom to Assemble Peacefully (Clause 1(b))
- Right to hold peaceful meetings, processions, or gatherings.
- Restriction Example: Cannot block highways or damage public property.

3. Freedom to Form Associations or Unions (Clause 1(c))
- Right to form political parties, trade unions, or organizations.
Restriction Example: Cannot form an association that threatens public order or national security.

4. Freedom to Move Freely throughout India (Clause 1(d))
- Right to travel or reside anywhere in India.
Restriction Example: Travel can be restricted during emergencies or quarantine situations.

5. Freedom to Reside and Settle in any Part of India (Clause 1(e))
- Right to live in any state or region.
Restriction Example: Certain areas reserved for tribal communities may restrict settlement.

6. Freedom to Practice Any Profession, Occupation, Trade, or Business (Clause 1(g))
- Right to pursue any lawful profession or business.
Restriction Example: Cannot run a factory that pollutes a residential area.

2.4- Fundamental Rights under Articles 20 and 22.


Article 20 – Protection in respect of conviction for offences
Article 20 gives protection to persons accused of crimes.It applies to both citizens and non-citizens.

1. No Ex-Post Facto Law (Article 20(1))
• A person cannot be punished for an act which was not a crime at the time it was committed.
• A heavier punishment cannot be given than what was prescribed at the time of the offence.
• Example: If an act was not illegal in 2020 but made illegal in 2022, a person cannot be punished for doing it in 2020.

2. No Double Jeopardy (Article 20(2))
• A person cannot be punished twice for the same offence.
• This is based on the principle: “Nemo Debet Bis Vexari” (no one should be tried twice for the same offence).

3. No Self-Incrimination (Article 20(3))
• An accused person cannot be forced to give evidence against himself.
• It protects the right to remain silent.
• Applies only to a person who is formally accused of an offence.
Example 2:
If a person is accused of fraud and the police force him to write a statement admitting the crime, it violates Article 20(3). But taking his signature sample for comparison does not violate this Article.

Article 22 – Protection against Arrest and Detention
Article 22 provides safeguards to persons who are arrested.

Rights of a person arrested (Article 22(1) & 22(2)):
• The person must be informed of the grounds of arrest.
• The person has the right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of his choice.
• The person must be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours of arrest.
• Detention beyond 24 hours is not allowed without permission of a magistrate.

Preventive Detention (Article 22(3)–22(7))
• Preventive detention means detention without trial to prevent a person from committing a future crime.
• Article 22 provides limited safeguards in such cases.
• The detainee must be informed of the grounds of detention.
• The detainee has the right to make a representation against the detention.
• An Advisory Board must review detention beyond 3 months.

Difference between Article 20 and Article 22:
• Article 20 protects against unfair punishment.
• Article 22 protects against illegal arrest and detention.

Conclusion:
Articles 20 and 22 ensure fairness in criminal law. They protect individuals from misuse of state power and safeguard personal liberty.

2.5- Article 21 and 21A – Right to Life and Personal Liberty & Right to Education.


Article 21 – Right to Life and Personal Liberty

Meaning:
• It applies to both citizens and non-citizens.
• The State cannot take away a person’s life or liberty except by a fair, just and reasonable law.
• The Supreme Court has expanded Article 21 to include many rights necessary for a dignified life.

Important Rights included under Article 21:
• Right to live with human dignity.
• Right to privacy.
• Right to clean environment.
• Right to legal aid.
• Right to speedy trial.
• Right to livelihood.
• Right to health.
• Right to shelter.

Example:
If a person is kept in jail without a fair trial, it violates Article 21 because it affects his personal liberty.

Article 21A – Right to Education

Added by the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002.

Meaning:
• The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children.
• It applies to children between the age of 6 to 14 years.
• Education must be provided in the manner determined by law (Right to Education Act, 2009).

Free Education:
• No child should pay fees or charges that prevent him/her from completing elementary education.

Compulsory Education:
• It is the duty of the government to ensure that every child goes to school.

Example:
If a government school refuses admission to a 10-year-old child without valid reason, it violates Article 21A.

Conclusion:
Article 21 protects the right to live with dignity and personal freedom, while Article 21A ensures that every child receives basic education. Both Articles strengthen human rights and social justice in India.

MODULE 3
3.1- Article 23 and 24 – Right against Exploitation.


Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24)
These Articles protect people from exploitation and inhuman treatment.
They ensure dignity, equality and freedom.

Article 23 – Prohibition of Human Trafficking and Forced Labour

Meaning:
• Prohibits human trafficking (buying and selling of human beings).
• Prohibits begar (forced labour without payment).
• Prohibits all forms of forced labour.
• Applies to both citizens and non-citizens.

Human Trafficking:
• Selling or purchasing people for prostitution, slavery, or illegal activities.

Forced Labour:
• When a person is forced to work against his will.
• Even if payment is very low or below minimum wage, it can amount to forced labour.

Exception:
• The State can impose compulsory service for public purposes (like military service), but without discrimination based on religion, race, caste, or class.

Example:
If a landlord forces a poor worker to work without wages to repay a loan, it violates Article 23.

Article 24 – Prohibition of Child Labour

Meaning:
• Prohibits employment of children below 14 years of age.
• Applies to work in factories, mines, or hazardous employment.

Purpose:
• To protect children from physical and mental exploitation.
• To ensure children focus on education and healthy development.

Example:
If a factory employs a 12-year-old child to work with dangerous machines, it violates Article 24.

Difference between Article 23 and Article 24:
• Article 23 protects all persons from forced labour and trafficking.
• Article 24 specifically protects children from hazardous employment.

Conclusion:
Articles 23 and 24 protect human dignity by preventing exploitation of workers and children. They promote equality and social justice in society.

3.2- Article 25-28 – Right to Religion.

Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28)
These Articles guarantee religious freedom in India.
India is a secular state, which means the State has no official religion and treats all religions equally.

Article 25 – Freedom of Conscience and Free Profession, Practice and Propagation of Religion

Meaning:
• Every person has the freedom of conscience (right to believe or not believe).
• Right to profess (declare), practice (follow), and propagate (spread) religion.
• Applies to both citizens and non-citizens.
Example:
A person is free to wear religious symbols, but cannot disturb public peace in the name of religion.

Restrictions:
- Must not disturb public order (no riots or violence).
- Must not harm public health (rituals causing disease can be restricted).
- Must not violate morality (cruel or exploitative practices are banned).
Example: A religious procession cannot block traffic for hours or harm anyone.

Article 26 – Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

Meaning:
• Every religious denomination has the right to:
 • Establish and maintain religious institutions.
• Manage its own religious affairs.
• Own and acquire property.
• Administer property according to law.

Restriction:
• Subject to public order, morality and health.

Article 27 – Freedom from Payment of Taxes for Promotion of Religion

Meaning:
• No person can be forced to pay taxes for promoting or maintaining any particular religion.
• The government cannot use tax money to favor one religion.

Example:
The State cannot collect a special tax only to support one specific religion.

Article 28 – Freedom from Religious Instruction in Certain Educational Institutions

Meaning:
• No religious instruction shall be provided in educational institutions wholly funded by the State.
• In institutions administered by the State but established under a trust, religious instruction may be allowed.
• No person can be forced to attend religious instruction without consent.

Conclusion:
Articles 25–28 ensure religious freedom while maintaining public order and secularism. They protect both individual and group rights and maintain equality among religions in India.

3.3- Article 29 and 30 – Rights of Minorities.


Purpose:
• Protect cultural, educational, and linguistic rights of minorities in India.
• Ensure minorities can preserve their identity and run institutions.

Article 29 – Protection of Interests of Minorities

What it says:
• Any section of citizens with a distinct language, culture, or script has the right to preserve it.
• No citizen can be denied admission to educational institutions based on religion, language, or culture.

Example:
A Telugu-speaking community can run cultural programs to preserve their language.
A school cannot refuse a student admission just because he belongs to a religious or linguistic minority.

Article 30 – Right of Minorities to Establish and Administer Educational Institutions

What it says:
• Minorities (religious or linguistic) have the right to establish and run educational institutions of their choice.
• The State cannot discriminate against these institutions in granting aid.
• Applies to both government-aided and private institutions.

Example:
A Muslim community can establish a school teaching its religion and culture.
A Christian minority can run a college with its own administration and syllabus while following general education standards.

Key Points:
• Article 29 = Protects cultural and linguistic identity.
• Article 30 = Protects educational rights of minorities.
• These rights ensure minorities can maintain their distinct identity in India.

3.4- Article 32- Right to Constitutional Remedies and Public Interest Litigation


Purpose:
• Article 32 is called the “heart and soul” of the Indian Constitution (as per Dr. B.R. Ambedkar).
• It allows citizens to approach the Supreme Court directly if their fundamental rights are violated.

Right to Constitutional Remedies:
• Every citizen can move the Supreme Court by writ petition to enforce fundamental rights.
• Writs are special orders issued by the Court to protect rights.

Types of Writs:
1. Habeas Corpus – Protects against illegal detention.
Example: If a person is unlawfully arrested, Court can order release.
2. Mandamus – Orders a public authority to perform its duty.
Example: Court can order government to provide education to children.
3. Prohibition – Stops a lower court or authority from exceeding its powers.
Example: Court can prevent a tribunal from acting beyond its authority.
4. Certiorari – Quashes orders of a lower court or authority that violates law.
Example: Invalidating illegal government orders.
5. Quo Warranto – Questions the legality of a person holding public office.
Example: Court can ask a person holding government post whether they are legally entitled.

Public Interest Litigation (PIL):
• PIL allows any person or organization to file a case on behalf of those who cannot approach the Court themselves (e.g., poor, illiterate, disadvantaged).
• Makes justice accessible to all, not just the rich or powerful.

Example:
• Filing a PIL to stop a factory from polluting a river affecting nearby villagers.
• Filing a PIL for rights of children in orphanages or prisoners in jails.

Key Points:
• Article 32 protects all fundamental rights.
• Citizens can directly approach the Supreme Court.
• PIL makes justice socially inclusive.
• Supreme Court can issue writs to enforce rights and prevent abuse of power.

Conclusion:
Article 32 empowers citizens and promotes rule of law, justice, and accountability in India.

3.5– Saving of Certain Laws - Article 31, 33, 34, 35.


Purpose:
• These Articles protect certain laws from being declared invalid even if they conflict with Fundamental Rights.
• They ensure state stability and special powers in certain situations.

Article 31 – [Repealed]
• Earlier, Article 31 protected the right to property as a fundamental right.
• Repealed by the 44th Amendment Act, 1978.
• Now, right to property is a legal right under the Right to Property Act.

Article 33 – Power of Parliament to Modify Fundamental Rights for Armed Forces, etc.
• Parliament can restrict or modify certain fundamental rights of:
– Armed Forces
– Police
– Intelligence Services
• Purpose: Ensure discipline, security, and proper functioning.

Example:
• Soldiers may have restricted freedom of speech to maintain discipline in the army.

Article 34 – Laws Validating Certain Acts during Emergency or Martial Law
• Parliament can validate laws that would otherwise violate Fundamental Rights if:
– Emergency or martial law was in place
– Acts were done in good faith
• Protects the State from legal challenges for past actions during emergencies.

Example:
• If government confiscated property during emergency, Article 34 protects the law from being struck down.

Article 35 – Power of Parliament to Enforce Fundamental Rights
• Parliament can make laws to implement Fundamental Rights.
• Can provide penalties for violation of Fundamental Rights.
• Example: Laws related to equality, abolition of untouchability, or protection against exploitation.

Key Points:
• Articles 31–35 ensure some exceptions and protections for the State.
• Balance between individual rights and public interest.
• Article 31 is repealed, but 33–35 are still important for security and law enforcement.

Conclusion:
These Articles allow Parliament and the State to make special laws or protect certain laws even if they affect Fundamental Rights, maintaining national security, discipline, and public welfare.
