1.1 INTRODUCTION
History and nature of a contractual obligation
Contemporary Relevance



History of Contract Law
Before 1872, India followed English contract law.
To bring uniform rules for business, agreements and promises,
the British passed the Indian Contract Act, 1872.
It gives rules on how contracts are made, performed and enforced.

2. Nature of Contractual Obligation
Contractual obligation = Legal duty created by a contract.

Important points:
  1. A contract is created when:
    Offer + Acceptance + Consideration + Intention + Free Consent
  2. When a contract is formed, parties must legally perform what they promised.
  3. If someone breaks the promise, they must pay compensation.
  4. Contractual obligation is always voluntary (self-created).
  5. Once accepted, the promise becomes a binding legal duty.
Example:
A promises to repair B‚Äôs phone for ‚Çπ800.
Once B agrees, both have legal obligations.

Contract law is important in today‚Äôs modern life because almost everything works through contracts.

Why contract law matters today:

In short:

1.2 OFFER, ACCEPTANCE AND ITS COMMUNICATION


1. OFFER / PROPOSAL (Section 2(a))

Meaning:
When one person shows willingness to do something or not to do something,
with the intention to get the other person‚Äôs consent,
it is an offer.

Example:
A says to B: ‚ÄúI will sell my phone for ‚Çπ5,000.‚Äù ‚Üí Offer

Essentials of a valid offer:
  1. Must be clear and definite
  2. Must be made with intention to create legal obligation
  3. Must be communicated to the offeree
  4. Can be specific (to one person) or general (to the public).
  5. Cannot be based on silence.
  6. Terms must be certain.


    7. 2. ACCEPTANCE (Section 2(b))
    Meaning
    When the person to whom the offer is made agrees to it,
    he is said to have accepted the offer.

    Simple Example
    B says: ‚ÄúI agree to buy your phone for ‚Çπ5,000.‚Äù ‚Üí Acceptance.

    Essentials of Valid Acceptance

    1. Must be absolute and unconditional.
    2. Must be communicated to the offeror.
    3. Must be made in the manner prescribed in the offer.
    4. Must be given before offer lapses.
    5. Cannot be given under force, fraud or mistake.
    6. Acceptance must be given by the person to whom the offer is made.
    7. Silence does not mean acceptance.

    3. COMMUNICATION OF OFFER AND ACCEPTANCE (Sections 3 & 4)

    (A) Communication of Offer
    Offer is communicated when it comes to the knowledge of the offeree.
    Example:
    A sends a letter offering to sell a bike. B must read it or know about it.

    (B) Communication of Acceptance
    There are two perspectives:

    1. As against the proposer (offeror)
    Acceptance is complete when it is posted / sent.
    Example:
    B posts a letter accepting A‚Äôs offer ‚Üí acceptance complete against A.

    2. As against the acceptor
    Acceptance is complete when it reaches the proposer.
    Example:
    A receives the letter ‚Üí acceptance complete against B.

    4. COMMUNICATION OF REVOCATION (Section 5)

    Revocation of Offer
    Offer can be taken back anytime before acceptance is posted/sent.

    Revocation of Acceptance
    Acceptance can be cancelled anytime before it reaches the proposer.

    ‚≠ê 5. MODES OF COMMUNICATION (Section 3)

    Communication can be:
    Spoken
    Written
    Electronic (email, WhatsApp)
    By conduct actions(nodded head)

    6. CASE LAWS

    Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.
    General offer to the public can be accepted by anyone who performs the condition.

    Felthouse v. Bindley
    Silence does not amount to acceptance.

    Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt
    For acceptance of a general offer, the person must know about the offer.

    ‚≠ê WHAT IS FREE CONSENT? (Section 14)

    2.4 Consideration



    WHAT IS CONSIDERATION? (Sec. 2(d))
    Simple Meaning:
    Something in return.
    Each party must give something and get something.

    Examples:
    You pay money ‚Üí shopkeeper gives goods
    You teach ‚Üí student pays fee
    You promise something ‚Üí other party promises something

    Without consideration = no contract (Sec. 25).

    FEATURES OF VALID CONSIDERATION

    1. Must move at the desire of the promisor
      Your act must be done because the promisor wanted it.

      Example:
      A promises to pay B if B paints A‚Äôs house ‚Üí valid.
      But B paints without A asking ‚Üí not valid.

    2. Can move from promisee or any other person
      Means third party can also give consideration.

      Example:
      A promises to pay B if C performs some work ‚Üí valid.

    3. May be past, present, or future

      Past Consideration:
      Already done, now promised.
      Example: A helps B, later B promises ‚Çπ1000.

      Present (Executed) Consideration:
      Payment or delivery done immediately.
      Example: Cash sale.

      Future (Executory) Consideration:
      Promise to do something later.
      Example: Order online, delivery after 3 days.

    FEATURES OF VALID CONSIDERATION (Continued)

    1. Need not be adequate
      Courts do NOT check if consideration is equal or fair.
      Example:
      Selling phone worth ‚Çπ20,000 for ‚Çπ500 ‚Üí valid (if free consent).

    2. Must be real, not imaginary
      Impossible or uncertain consideration is not valid.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúI will give you ‚Çπ10,000 if you catch a star‚Äù ‚Üí invalid.

    3. Must be lawful
      Cannot be illegal, immoral, or against public policy.

    2.5 Unlawful Object and Consideration



    SECTION 23 ‚Äì WHAT MAKES OBJECT / CONSIDERATION UNLAWFUL?

    Object = purpose of the contract
    Consideration = what each party gives/gets

    A contract is VOID if the object or consideration is:
    Forbidden by law
    Illegal
    Fraudulent
    Injury to person or property
    Immoral
    Against public policy

    If any one of these applies ‚Üí the agreement becomes VOID.

    1. FORBIDDEN BY LAW
      If law directly prohibits it, contract is void.
      Examples:
      Selling drugs without license
      Hiring a killer
      Doing business without required government license

    2. NATURE OF CONTRACT IS ILLEGAL
      If the contract itself is illegal.
      Example:
      Agreement to smuggle gold from another country.

    TYPES OF VOID AGREEMENTS

    1. Agreement by a Minor ‚Üí Void
      Contract with a minor (below 18) is void.
      Minor cannot be forced to perform.
      Example:
      A (17 years old) promises to buy a bike ‚Üí Void.

    2. Agreement Without Consideration (Sec. 25) ‚Üí Void
      If there is no consideration, it is void (except a few special cases).
      Example:
      ‚ÄúI will give you ‚Çπ10,000 for free‚Äù ‚Üí Void.

    3. Agreements in Restraint of Marriage ‚Üí Void
      No one can restrict someone from marrying.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúYou must not marry for 5 years‚Äù ‚Üí Void.

    4. Agreements in Restraint of Trade ‚Üí Void
      You cannot stop someone from doing a business or profession.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúYou cannot open a shop in this city‚Äù ‚Üí Void.
      (Except partnership goodwill limits)

    5. Agreements in Restraint of Legal Proceedings ‚Üí Void
      No one can stop someone from going to court.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúYou cannot sue me for anything‚Äù ‚Üí Void.

    6. Uncertain Agreements (Sec. 29) ‚Üí Void
      If terms are unclear, vague, or not certain, it is void.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúI will sell you something at a reasonable price‚Äù ‚Üí What price? ‚Üí Void.

    7. Wagering Agreements ‚Üí Void
      Betting or gambling is void.
      Example:
      Betting on cricket match ‚Üí Void.

    8. Impossible Agreements (Sec. 56) ‚Üí Void
      If performance is impossible, it is void.
      Example:
      ‚ÄúI will bring stars from the sky‚Äù ‚Üí Void.

    9. Agreement to Do an Unlawful Act ‚Üí Void
      Anything illegal is void.

    3.1 Performance of Contract



    WHAT IS PERFORMANCE?
    Performance = doing what you promised in the contract.
    A contract is completed only when both parties perform their obligations.

    TYPES OF PERFORMANCE

    1. Actual Performance
      The promisor fully completes the promise.
      Contract ends.
      Example:
      Seller delivers the goods ‚Üí Buyer pays ‚Üí Contract completed.

    2. Attempted Performance / Tender (Sec. 38)
      The promisor offers to perform, but the promisee refuses to accept.

      In this case, the promisor is not liable anymore.
      Example:
      Delivery boy brings goods ‚Üí Customer refuses.
      Performance attempted ‚Üí Promisor free.

    WHO MUST PERFORM? (Sec. 40 ‚Äì 45)

    1. Promisor Himself
      If the contract is based on personal skill
      (e.g. painter, singer, doctor).
      Only the promisor must perform.

    2. Agent
      If personal skill is not needed, performance can be done by an agent.

    3. Legal Representatives
      If promisor dies, legal representatives must perform.
      If personal skill contract ‚Üí ends.

    Other Contracts
    Legal heirs must perform.

    Joint Promisors (Sec. 42‚Äì45)
    All joint promisors are equally responsible.
    If one pays ‚Üí he can recover from others.

    TIME AND PLACE OF PERFORMANCE

    Time (Sec. 46‚Äì50)
    If time is fixed, the contract must be performed at that time.
    Delay can make contract voidable.
    Example:
    Deliver goods on 5th Dec ‚Üí Must deliver on that day.

    Place (Sec. 47‚Äì49)
    If place is not fixed, the promisor must ask the promisee where to perform.
    If place is fixed ‚Üí must perform there.

    PERFORMANCE OF RECIPROCAL PROMISES (Sec. 51‚Äì57)

    1. Mutual and Dependent Promises
      One promise depends on another.
      If A does not perform, B need not perform.

    2. Independent Promises
      Each must perform even if the other has not performed.

    3. Simultaneous Performance
      Both must perform at the same time.
      Example:
      Goods delivered ‚Üí payment made ‚Üí same moment.

    4. Impossible Promises (Sec. 56)
      Impossible acts are void.

    EFFECT OF REFUSAL TO PERFORM (Sec. 39)

    If one party refuses to perform, the other party can:
    End the contract
    Claim damages
    This is called anticipatory breach if refusal is before due date.

    WHEN PROMISEE MAY DISPENSE WITH PERFORMANCE (Sec. 63)

    Promisee can:
    Cancel performance
    Extend time
    Accept lesser performance
    Example:
    Accepting ‚Çπ7,000 instead of ‚Çπ10,000.

    3.2 Discharge of Contract



    WHAT IS DISCHARGE OF CONTRACT?
    Discharge = Contract ends.
    Parties are free from all legal obligations.

    WAYS IN WHICH CONTRACT IS DISCHARGED
    There are 6 main ways:

    1. DISCHARGE BY PERFORMANCE
      Most common.
      Both parties fully perform their promises ‚Üí Contract ends.
      Example:
      Seller delivers goods, buyer pays ‚Üí Over.

    2. DISCHARGE BY AGREEMENT (Sec. 62‚Äì67)
      Parties mutually agree to end or change the contract.

      A. Novation
      Old contract replaced by a new one.
      B. Rescission
      Contract is cancelled by agreement.
      C. Alteration
      Terms are changed but parties remain same.
      D. Remission
      Promisee accepts less performance.
      (e.g. Accepting ‚Çπ8,000 instead of ‚Çπ10,000)
      E. Waiver
      Promisee gives up rights.
      F. Accord & Satisfaction
      New agreement settles old dispute.

    3. DISCHARGE BY IMPOSSIBILITY (Sec. 56)
      Contract ends when performance becomes impossible.

      A. Initial Impossibility
      Impossible from the start ‚Üí Void.
      B. Subsequent Impossibility
      Becomes impossible later ‚Üí Contract ends.
      Examples:
      Death in personal skill contract
      Subject matter destroyed (house burns)
      Change in law makes performance illegal
      War or natural disaster

    DISCHARGE BY LAPSE OF TIME
    If a party does not take action within 3 years (Limitation Act), the contract automatically ends.
    Example:
    Debt not claimed for 3 years ‚Üí No legal recovery.

    DISCHARGE BY OPERATION OF LAW
    Contract ends automatically due to law.
    Includes:
    Death (in personal skill contracts)
    Insolvency (bankruptcy)
    Merger (when inferior right merges into superior right)
    Material alteration without consent

    DISCHARGE BY BREACH
    One party refuses or fails to perform ‚Üí Contract ends.

    3.3 Breach of Contract



    1. Types of Breach

    1. Actual Breach
      Happens on the due date or during performance.
      Example:
      You promise to deliver goods on 10 Jan but don‚Äôt deliver.

    2. Anticipatory Breach
      Happens before the due date when one party clearly says ‚ÄúI won‚Äôt perform‚Äù.
      Example:
      Delivery due on 10 Jan, but on 1 Jan you say ‚ÄúI cannot deliver‚Äù.

      Other party can:
      End contract immediately OR
      Wait till due date.

    2. How Breach Happens

    Non-performance
    Not doing what was promised.
    Example:
    Not supplying goods.

    Defective Performance
    Doing it wrongly.
    Example:
    Supplying damaged goods.

    Late Performance
    Doing it after the agreed time (if time is essential).

    3. Remedies for Breach (What the aggrieved party can claim)

    1. Damages (Money Compensation)

      a) Ordinary / General Damages
      For direct loss due to breach.
      Example:
      Market price difference.

      b) Special Damages
      For special losses known to both parties at the time of contract.
      Example:
      Supplier knew delay would cause loss of resale profit.

      c) Punitive / Exemplary Damages
      Rare.
      Given to punish the defaulter.
      Example:
      Breach of promise to marry.

      d) Nominal Damages
      Very small amount.
      Given when breach occurred but no actual loss.

    2. Specific Performance
      Court orders the party to perform exactly what was promised.
      Used when:
      Subject matter is unique (land, rare items).
      Money compensation is not sufficient.

    3. Injunction

    3.4 Types of Damages & Remedies for Breach


    ‚≠ê A. TYPES OF DAMAGES

    1. Ordinary / General Damages
      Given for direct loss caused by breach.
      Naturally arise in the normal course of events.
      Example:
      Seller fails to deliver goods ‚Üí buyer buys from market at higher price ‚Üí difference paid by seller.

    2. Special Damages
      Loss arising from special circumstances.
      Such circumstances must be known to both parties at the time of contract.
      Example:
      Seller knew delay would cause buyer to lose resale profit ‚Üí seller pays that profit loss.

    3. Exemplary / Punitive Damages
      Rare in contract law.
      Given to punish the wrongdoer.
      Examples:
      Breach of promise to marry.
      Wrongful dishonour of cheque by bank even when sufficient funds exist.

    4. Nominal Damages
      Very small amount of damages.
      Given when breach occurs but no actual loss is suffered.
      Example:
      Contract broken but no financial loss ‚Üí court may award ‚Çπ1 or small amount.

    5. Liquidated Damages
      Amount pre-decided and written in the contract.
      Court awards reasonable amount, not excessive.

      Example:
      Contract states ‚Äú‚Çπ10,000 for delay‚Äù ‚Üí court grants fair amount depending on loss.

    6. Unliquidated Damages
      Amount not fixed in the contract.
      Court decides damages under Section 73 of Indian Contract Act.

    7. Remote / Indirect Damages
      Loss not foreseeable at the time of contract.
      Such damages are NOT awarded by the court.

      Example:
      Loss that is too remote or unexpected.

    MODULE 4
    4.1 Origin of Specific Relief as Equitable Relief



    ‚≠ê Meaning of Specific Relief
    Specific Relief means the court orders the exact performance of what was promised in the contract.
    Note:
    It is not a punishment.
    It is a correction of a wrong.
    Example:
    If a person takes advance money but refuses to give the house,
    the court orders that person to perform the contract and transfer the house.

    ‚≠ê 2. Origin of Specific Relief (How it Started)

    (a) Origin in English Equity Courts

    Before modern laws, England had two types of courts:

    Common Law Courts
    These courts awarded damages (money compensation only).

    Equity Courts (Chancery Courts)

    These courts provided fair remedies such as:
    Specific Performance
    Injunction
    Rescission

    Damages were not sufficient in cases involving land, unique items, or trust property.
    Therefore, Equity Courts developed the concept of Specific Relief to ensure fairness.

    (b) Basis of Specific Relief ‚Äì ‚ÄúEquity and Good Conscience‚Äù

    Equity Courts acted on principles of:
     Fairness
    Justice
    Good conscience
    Reasonableness
    These principles became the foundation of Specific Relief law.

    (c) Adoption in India
    India followed English legal principles during British rule.
    Originally, Specific Relief was part of Contract and Civil Law.

    In 1877, the Specific Relief Act, 1877 was enacted based on English equity principles.
    In 1963, the law was updated as the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (present law).

    ‚≠ê 3. Why Specific Relief Was Needed?
    Because money compensation is not always adequate.

    Examples:
    Land or property cannot be replaced by money.
    Rare painting or unique item cannot be compensated by damages.
    Sometimes a person must be stopped from breaking a contract (injunction).

    Therefore, courts grant equitable relief instead of only damages.

    ‚≠ê 4. Nature of Specific Relief

    Discretionary ‚Äì granted at the discretion(way as to avoid causing offence) of the court.
    Equitable ‚Äì based on justice and good conscience.
    Corrective ‚Äì aims to restore the injured party‚Äôs rights.

    ‚≠ê 5. Key Features of Specific Relief

    Based on English Equity System.
    Developed because damages were not sufficient in many cases.
    Adopted into Indian law through the Specific Relief Act, 1877.
    Later updated as the Specific Relief Act, 1963.
    Provides remedies like:
    Specific Performance
    Injunctions
    Rescission
    Rectification
    Cancellation of instruments

    4.2 KINDS OF RELIEF UNDER SPECIFIC RELIEF ACT, 1963

    The Act provides different remedies when a civil right is violated.
    These remedies are based on:
    Equity
    Justice
    Good conscience

    4.2.1 POSSESSORY REMEDIES (Recovery of Possession)

    Used when someone unlawfully takes or keeps another person‚Äôs property.

    Includes:
    Recovery of movable property (Section 7).
    Recovery of immovable property (Section 5 & 6).

    Key Point:
    Even a person in wrongful possession can recover property from a later wrongdoer,
    because law protects possession.

    Example:
    A tenant is illegally thrown out ‚Üí tenant can file a suit to restore possession.

    4.2.2 SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE OF CONTRACTS

    Court orders a party to perform the exact terms of the contract.

    Given when:
    Contract is valid and enforceable.
    Subject matter is unique.
    Money compensation is not adequate.
    like:Contracts dependent on personal skills
    Example: singer, painter, actor.

    4.2.3 CONTRACTS THAT CANNOT BE SPECIFICALLY PERFORMED
    Court will NOT order specific performance for:
    • Contracts dependent on personal skills
      Example: singer, painter, actor.
    • Contracts involving continuous duties
      Example: running a business daily.
    • Contracts that are uncertain
      Example: vague terms like ‚Äúbest quality‚Äù.
    • Contracts where performance is impossible.
    • Contracts made by persons who cannot be compelled
      Example: minors, mentally unsound persons.
    In these cases, remedy is damages, not specific performance.

    4.2.4 SUBSTITUTED PERFORMANCE OF CONTRACT (Section 20)

    If one party breaks the contract, the aggrieved party can:
    • Get the work done by a third party, and
    • Recover the cost from the defaulting party.

    Note: No need to wait for court order.
    But ‚Üí must give 30-day notice before substituted performance.

    Example: A contractor does not build a wall ‚Üí owner hires another and claims cost.

    4.2.5 RECTIFICATION OF INSTRUMENTS (Section 26)

    Fixing mistakes in written documents (contract, deed) so it matches the real intention.
    Used when:
    • There is fraud, or
    • A mutual mistake of both parties.
    Example: Sale deed wrongly writes 100 sq ft instead of 1000 sq ft ‚Üí court rectifies.

    4.2.6 RESCISSION OF CONTRACT (Section 27)

    Court cancels the contract when:
    • Contract was obtained by fraud/misrepresentation
    • Contract is voidable
    • One party refuses to perform
    Effect: Both parties return any benefit ‚Üí status restored.

    4.2.7 CANCELLATION OF INSTRUMENT (Section 31)

    Court cancels a document that is:
    • Void or voidable, or
    • May cause harm if left outstanding
    Purpose: prevent future mischief or litigation.
    Example: Forged deed, illegal agreement ‚Üí court cancels.

    4.2.8 DECLARATORY DECREE (Section 34)

    Court issues a declaration regarding a legal right or status.
    Used when a person wants clarity about what is legally correct.
    Examples:
    • Declaring who is the real owner of property
    • Declaring a document void
    • Declaring legal status (marriage, adoption)
    No damages are awarded ‚Üí only declaration.

    4.2.9 INJUNCTIONS (Sections 36‚Äì42)

    court orders someone:
    ‚û° To do something ‚Üí Mandatory Injunction
    Example: Remove a wall built illegally.
    ‚û° Not to do something ‚Üí Prohibitory Injunction
    Example: Stop construction on disputed land.

    • Temporary injunction (short time, during case)
